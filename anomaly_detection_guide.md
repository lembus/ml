## 0. Preliminaries: What Is an Anomaly?

Before diving into techniques, we need a shared vocabulary.

An **anomaly** (or **outlier**) is an observation that deviates so significantly from the majority of the data that it arouses suspicion of being generated by a different mechanism. This framing — due to Hawkins (1980) — is deliberately generative: anomalies are not merely "rare," they are samples we believe come from a *different distribution* than the bulk of the data.

Three canonical types:

- **Point anomalies.** A single observation is unusual in isolation (e.g., a $10,000 charge on a card whose typical transactions are $50).
- **Contextual anomalies.** An observation is normal globally but unusual given its context (e.g., 30°C is fine in July, anomalous in January).
- **Collective anomalies.** A group of observations is unusual even though individuals aren't (e.g., a sustained flatline in an EKG — any single flat reading is fine, but dozens in a row is pathological).

Three learning paradigms:

- **Supervised.** Labels for normal/anomalous are available. Usually reduces to extremely imbalanced classification.
- **Semi-supervised.** Only normal data is available at training time; anomalies are flagged by deviation from the learned "normal" model.
- **Unsupervised.** No labels at all. The algorithm must decide what counts as deviation based purely on the data's geometry or density.

Most methods in this guide are **unsupervised or semi-supervised**, because in practice anomalies are rare, diverse, and expensive to label.

A recurring theme: **every anomaly detector is, implicitly or explicitly, a model of "normal."** Z-scores model normal as a Gaussian; Isolation Forest models normal as dense regions of feature space; autoencoders model normal as a low-dimensional manifold. An anomaly is anything the model explains poorly.

## 1. Statistical Methods: Z-score, Modified Z-score, IQR

### 1.1 Motivation & Intuition

Suppose you run a server and record daily request counts: mostly between 900 and 1,100 per day. One day you see 50,000. You don't need a neural network to flag this — it's *many standard deviations* above the mean. That intuition is the heart of statistical anomaly detection.

**Why does this topic exist?** Before computers, statisticians needed cheap, interpretable rules to flag suspect measurements in small datasets (lab experiments, census data). These rules survive today because they are:

- **Transparent.** A non-technical stakeholder understands "more than 3σ from the mean."
- **Fast.** $O(n)$ time and $O(1)$ memory per feature.
- **Effective as baselines.** For univariate, roughly-Gaussian data, they're hard to beat.

**Concrete real-world ML use cases:**

- **Data quality monitoring.** Before a model retrains, flag columns whose mean or variance has shifted too far from historical norms.
- **Feature drift detection.** Score incoming feature values against a reference distribution and page if the Z-score exceeds a threshold.
- **Pre-processing for downstream models.** Winsorize or remove extreme values so a linear model's coefficients aren't dominated by outliers.
- **Simple fraud triage.** First-pass filters in transaction pipelines before the expensive ML model runs.

### 1.2 Conceptual Foundations

**Key objects.**

- **Central tendency.** A summary of where the data "lives": mean $\mu$ or median.
- **Dispersion.** A summary of how spread out the data is: standard deviation $\sigma$, MAD (median absolute deviation), or IQR (interquartile range).
- **Threshold.** A cutoff beyond which a point is declared anomalous (e.g., $|z| > 3$).

**How they interact.**

1. Fit a central tendency and a dispersion statistic to a reference sample (either the full dataset or a clean "normal" window).
2. For each new point, compute a *standardized score* — how many "dispersion units" it sits from the center.
3. Compare the score against a pre-chosen threshold.

**Assumptions.**

- **Unimodality.** The data has a single "typical" regime. If you have a mixture of two populations (e.g., weekday vs weekend traffic), the mean sits in the low-density region between them, and *normal* points in each mode look anomalous.
- **Roughly symmetric or known-skew distribution.** The Z-score implicitly assumes the Gaussian bell shape. Heavy-tailed or skewed data invalidates the 3σ heuristic.
- **Stationarity.** The reference $\mu, \sigma$ used at scoring time must reflect the *current* process. If the mean shifts, every new point looks anomalous.
- **Univariate.** These methods operate one feature at a time. A point that is normal on each axis separately can be anomalous jointly (e.g., height=6'5", weight=80 lbs — each fine, together absurd).

**What breaks when assumptions fail.**

- **Multimodal data:** the Z-score flags normal points and misses truly anomalous values sitting near the gap between modes.
- **Heavy tails (e.g., financial returns):** with a Student-$t$-like distribution, 3σ events happen far more often than the Gaussian says, flooding alerts.
- **Non-stationarity:** a Z-score computed on last month's mean produces false alarms when the process legitimately shifted.
- **Outliers in the reference set:** the mean and σ themselves get distorted, making the detector blind to further outliers — the so-called **masking effect**.

### 1.3 Mathematical Formulation

#### 1.3.1 The Z-score

Let $x_1, \dots, x_n$ be a sample. Define

$$
\hat\mu = \frac{1}{n} \sum_{i=1}^n x_i, \qquad
\hat\sigma = \sqrt{\frac{1}{n-1} \sum_{i=1}^n (x_i - \hat\mu)^2}.
$$

The **Z-score** of a new observation $x$ is

$$
z(x) = \frac{x - \hat\mu}{\hat\sigma}.
$$

**Intuition of each term.** $(x - \hat\mu)$ is the raw deviation from the center; dividing by $\hat\sigma$ converts it to dimensionless "standard-deviation units" so the same threshold applies across features of different scales.

**Why the Gaussian link matters.** If $x \sim \mathcal{N}(\mu, \sigma^2)$, then $z \sim \mathcal{N}(0,1)$ and

$$
\Pr(|z| > 3) \approx 0.0027.
$$

Hence "$|z| > 3$" corresponds to roughly a 1-in-370 event under a Gaussian model. If the Gaussian assumption is wrong, the *meaning* of the threshold changes, even though the number doesn't.

**Breakdown point.** Both $\hat\mu$ and $\hat\sigma$ have a breakdown point of $0\%$: a single extreme contaminant can drag them arbitrarily. This is the root cause of masking.

#### 1.3.2 The Modified Z-score

To gain robustness we replace $\hat\mu$ with the **median** and $\hat\sigma$ with the **median absolute deviation (MAD)**:

$$
\text{MAD} = \operatorname{median}_i |x_i - \operatorname{median}(x)|.
$$

The MAD is scaled so that, *for Gaussian data*, it is a consistent estimator of $\sigma$:

$$
\hat\sigma_{\text{MAD}} = \frac{\text{MAD}}{\Phi^{-1}(0.75)} \approx \frac{\text{MAD}}{0.6745},
$$

where $\Phi^{-1}(0.75) \approx 0.6745$ is the 75th percentile of the standard normal. (Derivation: for $X \sim \mathcal{N}(0,\sigma^2)$, $|X|$ has median $\sigma \Phi^{-1}(0.75)$; setting sample median $|X|$ equal to this and solving gives the scaling.)

Iglewicz and Hoaglin's **modified Z-score** is then

$$
M_i = \frac{0.6745 \, (x_i - \operatorname{median}(x))}{\text{MAD}},
$$

with $|M_i| > 3.5$ as a common cutoff. The constant 0.6745 ensures $M_i$ is on roughly the same scale as a standard Z under Gaussianity.

**Breakdown point.** The median has a $50\%$ breakdown point — up to half the data can be arbitrarily corrupted without moving it beyond a bounded amount. MAD is similarly robust.

#### 1.3.3 The IQR rule

Define the first and third quartiles $Q_1$ and $Q_3$, and the **interquartile range** $\text{IQR} = Q_3 - Q_1$. Tukey's rule flags any point outside

$$
[Q_1 - 1.5 \cdot \text{IQR}, \quad Q_3 + 1.5 \cdot \text{IQR}]
$$

as a **mild outlier**, and outside $[Q_1 - 3 \cdot \text{IQR}, Q_3 + 3 \cdot \text{IQR}]$ as a **far outlier** (the basis of box-plot whiskers).

**Where does 1.5 come from?** For $X \sim \mathcal{N}(\mu, \sigma^2)$, $Q_3 - Q_1 \approx 1.349\sigma$. Setting the upper fence at $Q_3 + 1.5 \cdot \text{IQR}$ places it at approximately

$$
\mu + 0.6745\sigma + 1.5 \cdot 1.349\sigma \approx \mu + 2.7\sigma,
$$

so under Gaussianity the rule flags about $0.7\%$ of observations per tail — a sensible "unusual but not catastrophic" cutoff. Tukey chose 1.5 empirically; 3 corresponds to roughly $\mu + 4.7\sigma$, an "extreme" cutoff.

### 1.4 Worked Examples

**Dataset.** Daily processing latency (ms) for a web service:

```
[102, 98, 110, 105, 101, 99, 103, 107, 104, 100, 900]
```

**Z-score approach.**

- $n = 11$, $\hat\mu = \frac{102 + 98 + \dots + 900}{11} = \frac{1929}{11} \approx 175.4$.
- $\hat\sigma \approx 240.6$ (dominated by the 900).
- Z-score for 900: $z = (900 - 175.4) / 240.6 \approx 3.01$. Just above 3 — flagged, but barely.
- Z-score for 102 (a normal point): $z = (102 - 175.4) / 240.6 \approx -0.31$. Fine.
- **Masking in action.** If we had two outliers (say 900 and 850), the mean and σ inflate further; neither may clear a 3σ bar even though both are clearly abnormal.

**Modified Z-score approach.**

- Median $= 103$.
- Absolute deviations from median: $\{1, 5, 7, 2, 2, 4, 0, 4, 1, 3, 797\}$. Median of these = $3$.
- $M$ for 900: $0.6745 \cdot (900 - 103) / 3 \approx 179.2$. Vastly above 3.5 — strongly flagged.
- $M$ for 102: $0.6745 \cdot (102 - 103) / 3 \approx -0.22$. Fine.
- The robust version shrugs off the contaminant.

**IQR approach.**

- Sorted: $\{98, 99, 100, 101, 102, 103, 104, 105, 107, 110, 900\}$.
- $Q_1 = 100$, $Q_3 = 107$, $\text{IQR} = 7$.
- Mild outlier fence: $[100 - 10.5, 107 + 10.5] = [89.5, 117.5]$.
- 900 is far outside — flagged. All normal points lie inside.

The robust methods agree that 900 is the anomaly; the classical Z-score is noisy and nearly misses it.

### 1.5 Relevance to Machine Learning Practice

**Where these methods appear in real systems.**

- **Training-time data cleaning.** Before fitting a regression, apply MAD-based clipping per feature.
- **Monitoring pipelines.** Services like Prometheus, Datadog, and internal ML platforms use Z-score or IQR on rolling windows of metrics (latency, error rate, feature means) to fire alerts.
- **Feature drift detection.** Compute $\mu, \sigma$ on a reference window, Z-score production values, and flag features whose distribution shifts significantly.
- **Label noise detection.** On residuals from a fitted model, extreme Z-scores can indicate mislabeled examples.

**When to use.**

- Roughly unimodal, roughly symmetric univariate data.
- Need for an explainable, auditable threshold.
- Compute or memory is constrained (streaming scenarios where only running $\mu, \sigma$ can be maintained).

**When not to use.**

- Multivariate anomalies where joint structure matters.
- Heavy-tailed or multimodal data.
- Non-stationary streams unless $\mu, \sigma$ are continuously updated (e.g., EWMA).

**Alternatives.**

- For multivariate: Mahalanobis distance (accounts for covariance), density methods.
- For heavy tails: rank-based (quantile) thresholds.
- For non-stationary: CUSUM, EWMA control charts, change-point methods (§7).

**Trade-offs.**

- **Bias–variance.** Robust estimators (median, MAD) have higher variance under truly Gaussian data (asymptotic relative efficiency of median vs. mean is $2/\pi \approx 64\%$) but dramatically lower bias under contamination.
- **Interpretability.** Highest among all methods in this guide.
- **Robustness.** Z-score: very fragile. Modified Z / IQR: robust.
- **Cost.** $O(n)$ for all three, though MAD and IQR require sorting or selection ($O(n)$ or $O(n \log n)$).

### 1.6 Common Pitfalls & Misconceptions

- **"3σ means 0.27% false-positive rate."** Only under Gaussianity. For heavy-tailed data it can be 5% or more, flooding alerts.
- **Using the mean to remove outliers.** The outliers corrupt the mean (masking), defeating the purpose. Use the median-based version for iterative cleaning.
- **Applying Z-score to multimodal data.** Bimodal distributions of, say, weekday/weekend traffic produce a mean in the trough between modes. Every normal point gets flagged. Fix: segment by context first.
- **Stale statistics.** Computing $\mu, \sigma$ once at deployment and never updating. Over time, natural drift causes either alarm floods or total blindness. Use rolling windows or exponentially weighted statistics.
- **Confusing MAD with mean absolute deviation.** The textbook MAD uses the median; the "mean absolute deviation" uses the mean and is non-robust. They share an acronym and cause endless confusion.
- **Forgetting the scale factor 1/0.6745.** Students sometimes compare raw MAD to a 3σ threshold; this under-flags because MAD $\approx 0.6745\sigma$ for Gaussian data.
- **Thinking IQR 1.5× is derived from a strict probability.** It's a convention that approximates a 2.7σ cutoff under Gaussianity, not a law.

### 1.7 Interview Preparation — Statistical Methods

#### Foundational

**Q1. Define the Z-score and explain what threshold you'd use.**

*Answer.* $z = (x - \mu)/\sigma$. The common threshold is $|z| > 3$, which under a Gaussian assumption corresponds to roughly a 0.27% two-tailed tail probability. Importantly, the meaning of this threshold depends on the distribution; for heavy-tailed data, 3σ events are far more common than 0.27%.

**Q2. Why might you prefer the modified Z-score over the standard one?**

*Answer.* The standard Z-score uses $\mu$ and $\sigma$, both of which have a breakdown point of $0\%$: a single extreme contaminant distorts them. This causes *masking* — additional outliers look normal because the scale statistic is inflated. The modified Z-score uses the median and MAD, which have $50\%$ breakdown, so they resist contamination.

**Q3. Explain the 1.5 × IQR rule conceptually.**

*Answer.* The IQR captures the middle 50% of the distribution. Tukey's rule says: anything more than 1.5 × IQR beyond the quartiles is a mild outlier, 3 × IQR beyond is a far outlier. Under Gaussianity the 1.5 cutoff sits at about 2.7σ, so it's conceptually a robust cousin of the 3σ rule. The key advantage is that quartiles are percentile-based and immune to extreme values.

#### Mathematical

**Q4. Derive the MAD-to-σ scaling constant.**

*Answer.* For $X \sim \mathcal{N}(0, \sigma^2)$, define $Y = |X - \mu|$. We want the median of $Y$. $\Pr(|X - \mu| \le t) = 2\Phi(t/\sigma) - 1 = 0.5$ gives $\Phi(t/\sigma) = 0.75$, so $t = \sigma \Phi^{-1}(0.75)$. Numerically $\Phi^{-1}(0.75) \approx 0.6745$. Thus the population MAD equals $0.6745\sigma$, and to estimate $\sigma$ we divide the sample MAD by 0.6745 — equivalently, multiply by $\approx 1.4826$.

**Q5. Show that the sample mean has breakdown point 0.**

*Answer.* Let $\bar x_n = \frac{1}{n}\sum x_i$. Replace $x_1$ with $M$; then $\bar x_n$ becomes $\bar x_n + (M - x_1)/n$, which tends to $\infty$ as $M \to \infty$. So a single contaminant can drive the mean arbitrarily far — breakdown = $1/n \to 0$.

**Q6. Under what distributional conditions do Z-score and IQR thresholds roughly coincide?**

*Answer.* Under Gaussianity with large $n$: $Q_3 - Q_1 \approx 1.349\sigma$, so $Q_3 + 1.5\text{IQR} \approx \mu + 0.6745\sigma + 2.024\sigma = \mu + 2.7\sigma$. A $|z| > 2.7$ rule and a 1.5 × IQR rule are approximately equivalent. They diverge for skewed or heavy-tailed distributions — IQR adapts to asymmetry (different fences above vs below), while the symmetric Z cutoff doesn't.

#### Applied

**Q7. You deploy a Z-score-based drift detector on production features. After a month, alerts fire constantly. What could be wrong?**

*Answer.* Several likely culprits, in order: (1) **non-stationarity** — the reference $\mu, \sigma$ are stale; fix with a rolling window. (2) **distribution is heavy-tailed or non-Gaussian** — true tail probability at $|z| > 3$ exceeds 0.27%; switch to quantile-based thresholds. (3) **multimodality** induced by segmentation (weekday/weekend) not captured in the reference; fix by conditioning on context. (4) **contamination in the reference window itself** — the $\sigma$ is inflated, causing under-alerting initially, then over-alerting after ${} \sigma$ is recomputed on cleaner data.

**Q8. Your team needs a per-feature drift score that is both interpretable and resistant to occasional contaminated batches. Which method do you pick and why?**

*Answer.* Modified Z-score based on a *rolling median and rolling MAD* over a reference window. It's interpretable (a number a PM can read), scale-free, and robust to the inevitable bad batches from upstream data issues. Pair with IQR-based secondary check for reporting to domain users who prefer percentile language.

#### Debugging & failure-mode

**Q9. A colleague reports that the Z-score detector never fires, even on obviously bad data. What's happening?**

*Answer.* Classic masking. The bad points inflate $\sigma$ so much that *nothing* — including those points themselves — clears the 3σ bar. Confirm by computing Z-scores with median-based estimators; if those fire but the mean-based don't, it's masking. Fix: iterative outlier removal, robust statistics, or trim before computing $\sigma$.

**Q10. Your modified Z-score detector returns `inf` or `NaN` for many points. Why?**

*Answer.* Almost certainly MAD = 0, which happens when more than half the values are identical (a discrete-valued or heavily-quantized feature). Division by zero produces infinity. Fixes: (a) add a tiny epsilon floor to the MAD, (b) switch to IQR if $Q_3 > Q_1$, or (c) handle that feature with a frequency-based detector instead.

#### Probing follow-ups

**Q11. Why not just use percentiles (e.g., flag anything above the 99th percentile)?**

*Answer.* You can — it's sometimes called a quantile-based detector. The trade-off: percentile cutoffs are robust and distribution-agnostic, but they have no notion of "how far beyond the percentile." A point at the 99.9th percentile and one 10× further out both get the same flag. Z-score-like methods provide a *magnitude* of anomaly, useful for ranking and prioritization.

**Q12. How would you combine the modified Z-score across multiple features into a single anomaly score?**

*Answer.* Two common approaches: (1) **max pooling** — anomaly score = $\max_j |M_j|$, good when any feature being abnormal suggests an anomaly. (2) **aggregation via sum-of-squares** — $\sum_j M_j^2$, which is a poor man's Mahalanobis distance assuming feature independence. For a proper multivariate treatment, use Mahalanobis with a robust covariance estimator (MCD, OGK). This also touches the ensemble topic in §6.

## 2. Distance-Based Methods: k-NN Distance and Local Outlier Factor

### 2.1 Motivation & Intuition

Statistical methods treat each feature independently and assume a simple parametric shape. But in many problems — image embeddings, customer behavior vectors, sensor grids — "normal" is a complicated, multi-dimensional cloud, and anomalies are *points that sit far from the cloud*. That intuition is the essence of distance-based detection: **a point is anomalous if its neighbors are far away**.

**Why this matters.** Consider fraud detection on 50 features about each transaction. A fraudulent transaction might not be extreme on any single feature but sits far from any cluster of legitimate transactions in the joint space. No univariate rule catches this. But "average distance to the 10 nearest neighbors" naturally does.

**The critical refinement — locality.** Suppose you have two clusters, one dense and one sparse. A point 5 units from the dense cluster is suspicious; a point 5 units from the sparse cluster is perfectly typical of that cluster. **Local Outlier Factor (LOF)** formalizes this: an anomaly score should compare a point's local density to the local density of its neighbors, not to a global baseline.

**Real-world ML use cases.**

- **Intrusion detection.** Log embeddings of network connections; flag points distant from known benign clusters.
- **Recommender system quality control.** User embeddings that sit unusually far from all peer groups.
- **Manufacturing QC.** Multivariate sensor vectors at points on an assembly line; flag units with neighbors very far from them.
- **Cold-start outlier screening.** Before a model is trained, remove points whose distance to any reasonable neighbor is huge.

### 2.2 Conceptual Foundations

**Key terms.**

- **Distance metric $d(\cdot,\cdot)$.** Usually Euclidean; sometimes cosine, Manhattan, or a learned metric. The metric *defines* what "close" means, so it defines what "anomalous" means.
- **$k$ — number of neighbors.** A hyperparameter controlling locality scale.
- **$k$-distance of $p$**, $d_k(p)$: the distance from $p$ to its $k$-th nearest neighbor. Large $d_k(p) \Rightarrow$ sparse neighborhood.
- **$k$-neighborhood $N_k(p)$**: the set of points within $d_k(p)$ of $p$ (so $|N_k(p)| \ge k$, with ties).
- **Reachability distance** (LOF): $\text{reach-dist}_k(p, o) = \max\{d_k(o), d(p, o)\}$. A smoothed version of distance that prevents tiny fluctuations from exploding the inverse density.
- **Local reachability density** (LOF): $\text{lrd}_k(p) = 1 / \overline{\text{reach-dist}_k(p, \cdot)}$. A smoothed local density estimate.
- **LOF score**: ratio of average neighbor lrd to $p$'s own lrd. 1 ≈ as dense as neighbors; >> 1 ≈ much less dense ⇒ anomaly.

**How k-NN distance works (simple version).**

1. For each point $p$, compute $d_k(p)$ (or average of distances to the $k$ nearest neighbors).
2. Rank all points by that score; large = anomalous.

**How LOF works.**

1. For each $p$, find $N_k(p)$.
2. Compute $\text{lrd}_k(p)$ — how dense is $p$'s neighborhood (from $p$'s view).
3. For each $p$, compute $\text{LOF}_k(p) = \frac{1}{|N_k(p)|} \sum_{o \in N_k(p)} \frac{\text{lrd}_k(o)}{\text{lrd}_k(p)}$.
4. LOF $\approx 1$: normal. LOF $\gg 1$: local anomaly.

**Assumptions.**

- The chosen metric is meaningful — distances actually reflect "dissimilarity."
- There is enough data for $k$ neighbors to give a reliable density estimate.
- Neighborhoods around inliers are locally roughly uniform (so lrd is meaningful).
- The relevant "anomalousness" is geometric — about position in feature space, not about temporal or relational context.

**What breaks.**

- **Curse of dimensionality.** In high dimensions, all pairwise distances concentrate around a similar value; $d_k(p)$ loses discriminative power.
- **Irrelevant or unscaled features.** A single large-scale feature dominates Euclidean distance, making the method effectively univariate on that axis.
- **Duplicates.** If $k$ or more points coincide with $p$, $d_k(p) = 0$, lrd $= \infty$ in LOF, breaking the ratio.
- **Variable-density normal regions** foil plain k-NN distance (a point in a sparse but normal region looks anomalous). LOF is designed to fix this.

### 2.3 Mathematical Formulation

#### 2.3.1 k-NN distance score

Given dataset $X = \{x_1, \dots, x_n\}$ and a query $p$ (possibly in $X$ or not), let $\pi(1), \dots, \pi(n)$ be the indices of $X$ sorted by $d(p, x_{\pi(i)})$. Then

$$
s_k(p) = d(p, x_{\pi(k)}) \quad \text{(single-kNN)} \quad \text{or} \quad s_k(p) = \frac{1}{k}\sum_{i=1}^{k} d(p, x_{\pi(i)}) \quad \text{(avg-kNN)}.
$$

Both are monotonic in "how isolated" $p$ is. Threshold or rank-select as desired.

**Connection to density.** For a uniform density $f$ on a region of $\mathbb{R}^d$, the expected $k$-th nearest-neighbor distance scales as $s_k \propto (k/(n f))^{1/d}$. Hence $s_k$ is an implicit estimator of $1/f^{1/d}$ — small $f$ (low density) gives large $s_k$. This grounds the method in density estimation even though it doesn't explicitly estimate $f$.

#### 2.3.2 Local Outlier Factor — full derivation

**Step 1: k-distance and k-neighborhood.**

$d_k(p)$ is defined so that:

- at least $k$ points $o \ne p$ satisfy $d(p, o) \le d_k(p)$, and
- at most $k-1$ points $o \ne p$ satisfy $d(p, o) < d_k(p)$.

So $d_k(p)$ is the distance to the $k$-th nearest neighbor (with tie-handling). The $k$-neighborhood is $N_k(p) = \{o : d(p, o) \le d_k(p)\}$, which has at least $k$ points.

**Step 2: Reachability distance.**

$$
\text{reach-dist}_k(p, o) = \max\{d_k(o), d(p, o)\}.
$$

The reachability distance from $p$ to $o$ is at least $o$'s own $k$-distance. Why? Because if $p$ is very close to $o$, using $d(p,o)$ directly would make $\text{lrd}(p)$ explode due to one neighbor. By flooring at $d_k(o)$, we smooth over tiny local fluctuations. This is a *statistical regularization* — essentially, reach-dist is $d(p, o)$ clipped from below at $o$'s neighborhood scale.

*Important asymmetry:* reach-dist is **not** a metric; $\text{reach-dist}_k(p, o) \ne \text{reach-dist}_k(o, p)$ in general.

**Step 3: Local reachability density.**

$$
\text{lrd}_k(p) = \frac{1}{\dfrac{\sum_{o \in N_k(p)} \text{reach-dist}_k(p, o)}{|N_k(p)|}}
= \frac{|N_k(p)|}{\sum_{o \in N_k(p)} \text{reach-dist}_k(p, o)}.
$$

Intuition: the reciprocal of the average reach-dist to $p$'s neighbors. Low reach-dists ⇒ dense region ⇒ large lrd.

**Step 4: LOF.**

$$
\text{LOF}_k(p) = \frac{1}{|N_k(p)|} \sum_{o \in N_k(p)} \frac{\text{lrd}_k(o)}{\text{lrd}_k(p)} = \frac{\frac{1}{|N_k(p)|}\sum_{o \in N_k(p)} \text{lrd}_k(o)}{\text{lrd}_k(p)}.
$$

This is the ratio of neighbors' average local density to $p$'s local density.

**Interpretation.**

- LOF $\approx 1$: $p$ has density similar to its neighbors — inlier.
- LOF $\gg 1$ (say 2+): $p$'s neighbors are much denser than $p$ — $p$ is a local outlier.
- LOF $< 1$: $p$ lies inside a cluster denser than its own neighborhood — rarely useful, typically ignored.

**Why the ratio.** If we used $1/\text{lrd}(p)$ alone, we'd detect global low density, losing the local context that motivates LOF. The ratio cancels absolute density, leaving "how much less dense am I than people around me."

#### 2.3.3 Complexity

- Naive: $O(n^2)$ for the pairwise distance matrix.
- With $k$-d trees or ball trees (low dimensions): $O(n \log n)$ on average.
- With approximate NN (HNSW, Annoy, FAISS): near-linear in $n$ even at high dimensions, at the cost of approximation error.

### 2.4 Worked Examples

**Toy dataset (2D).** Cluster A: $\{(0,0), (0,1), (1,0), (1,1)\}$. Cluster B (sparser): $\{(10,10), (10,13), (13,10)\}$. Query point $p = (5, 5)$ — midway between clusters, clearly anomalous.

**k-NN distance ($k=2$, Euclidean).**

- Distances from $p$: to $(1,1)$ = $\sqrt{32} \approx 5.66$; to $(0,1)$ = $\sqrt{41} \approx 6.40$; to $(10,10) = \sqrt{50} \approx 7.07$.
- $s_2(p) = 6.40$ (distance to second nearest).
- For a cluster-A point like $(0,0)$: neighbors at distance 1 and 1 ⇒ $s_2 = 1$.
- For a cluster-B point like $(10,10)$: neighbors at distance 3 and 3 ⇒ $s_2 = 3$.
- $p$'s score (6.40) is the highest — flagged. **But** cluster B points already have $s_2 = 3$, well above cluster A's 1. If we threshold at, say, 2, we would falsely flag all of cluster B. This is exactly the variable-density pathology LOF is built to solve.

**LOF ($k=2$) for the same data.**

- For a dense-cluster point like $(0,0)$: $d_2 = 1$, $N_2 = \{(0,1), (1,0)\}$, their $d_2$ values are also $\approx 1$. reach-dist $\approx 1$ throughout. lrd $\approx 1$. Neighbors' lrd also $\approx 1$. LOF $\approx 1$.
- For a sparse-cluster point like $(10,10)$: $d_2 = 3$, neighbors $(10,13)$ and $(13,10)$, each with $d_2 = 3$. reach-dist $\approx 3$. lrd $\approx 1/3$. Neighbors have lrd $\approx 1/3$. LOF $\approx 1$. **Critical insight:** LOF = 1 even though the cluster is sparse. LOF is *local* — sparseness is normal here.
- For $p = (5,5)$: nearest 2 are say $(1,1)$ at 5.66 and $(0,1)$ at 6.40. $d_2(p) = 6.40$. reach-dist$(p, (1,1)) = \max(d_2((1,1))=1, 5.66) = 5.66$; reach-dist$(p, (0,1)) = \max(1, 6.40) = 6.40$. lrd$(p) = 2 / (5.66 + 6.40) \approx 0.166$. Neighbors' lrd $\approx 1$. LOF$(p) \approx 1 / 0.166 \approx 6$. Strongly flagged.

LOF correctly says: cluster B is normal (LOF ≈ 1) despite being sparse; $p$ is anomalous (LOF ≈ 6).

### 2.5 Relevance to Machine Learning Practice

**When to use k-NN distance.**

- Low-ish dimensional data (say $d \lesssim 30$ after dimensionality reduction) where distance is meaningful.
- Roughly uniform "normal" density.
- You want a simple, interpretable score with a clear probabilistic backbone (density proxy).

**When to use LOF.**

- Normal data has multiple clusters with different densities.
- You care about *local* anomalies — points that stand out against their neighbors specifically.

**When not to use either.**

- Very high dimensions without dimensionality reduction (use autoencoders, §5, or tree-based methods, §3).
- Massive data without approximate NN infrastructure (runtime blows up).
- Streaming, real-time scoring (NN queries can be expensive unless you maintain a kd-tree index).

**Alternatives / complements.**

- Isolation Forest (§3) for high-dimensional, large datasets.
- One-class SVM (§3) when you want a learned decision boundary.
- Autoencoders (§5) for learned distance in a representation space.

**Trade-offs.**

- **Bias–variance.** Small $k$: low bias, high variance; a single neighbor dominates density estimate. Large $k$: smoother, more biased toward global density (losing locality).
- **Interpretability.** High — you can literally return the $k$ nearest neighbors and let a human inspect.
- **Robustness.** Moderate; a single extreme neighbor can skew $s_k$. LOF's reach-dist smoothing helps but not completely.
- **Computational cost.** Scales poorly in $n$ without indexing; storage of neighbor lists can be large.

### 2.6 Common Pitfalls & Misconceptions

- **Forgetting to scale features.** Euclidean distance is dominated by the largest-scale feature. Always standardize or use Mahalanobis if features have different units.
- **Choosing $k$ by default.** scikit-learn's default $k=20$ is not sacred. Rule of thumb: $k$ should be at least the size of the smallest cluster you'd call "normal," and small enough to reflect local structure. Sensitivity analysis across several $k$ is standard practice.
- **Running on raw high-dimensional data.** Distance concentration ruins the method. Project first (PCA, autoencoder) or use a metric better-suited to high dimensions (cosine for text embeddings).
- **Confusing LOF > 1 with global rarity.** LOF is *local*. A point in a genuinely sparse region can have LOF = 1 (normal) and still be globally rare. If global rarity matters, supplement with k-distance or density estimates.
- **Interpreting LOF as a probability.** It is a ratio, not a calibrated probability. Use rank or percentile, or calibrate on held-out labeled data.
- **Including the query in its own neighbors.** When scoring training points, many implementations include $p$ itself (distance 0); check the library's behavior.
- **Forgetting LOF duplicates problem.** If duplicates exist, $d_k = 0$ and lrd is infinite. De-duplicate or use reach-dist with a small floor.

### 2.7 Interview Preparation — Distance-Based Methods

#### Foundational

**Q1. Explain the intuition behind LOF in one minute.**

*Answer.* LOF measures how much less dense a point is than its neighbors. It scores each point by comparing its local density to the average local density of its $k$ nearest neighbors. A value near 1 means typical; much larger than 1 means the point sits in a sparser region than the neighborhood around it — a local outlier. The ratio makes LOF robust to datasets with multiple clusters of different densities; a plain k-NN distance can't distinguish "sparse but normal" from "truly anomalous."

**Q2. What does the hyperparameter $k$ control in LOF?**

*Answer.* $k$ sets the scale of locality. Small $k$ makes LOF sensitive to micro-neighborhood fluctuations (high variance). Large $k$ averages over a wider region (more bias, less locality). It should be chosen so that the neighborhood can plausibly represent a "normal cluster" — a good heuristic is $k$ somewhere between 10 and the size of the smallest natural cluster. In practice, run LOF over a range of $k$ and take the max score (the original paper recommends this).

#### Mathematical

**Q3. Derive LOF from scratch and explain each component's purpose.**

*Answer.* (Walk through §2.3.2.) Key points: reach-dist smooths over small fluctuations by flooring $d(p,o)$ at $d_k(o)$; lrd inverts average reach-dist to turn distance into density; LOF takes a ratio to cancel the absolute density, capturing only *relative* sparseness.

**Q4. Why floor $d(p,o)$ at $d_k(o)$ rather than at $d_k(p)$?**

*Answer.* We want to regularize by the *neighbor's* notion of neighborhood, because that's whose density we're trying to compare to. If $o$ sits in a dense cluster ($d_k(o)$ is small), the floor is loose and $d(p,o)$ is used directly; if $o$ sits in a sparse cluster, we don't let a fluke tiny $d(p,o)$ inflate $p$'s apparent density — we acknowledge $o$'s typical scale. Flooring at $d_k(p)$ instead would collapse everything into $p$'s own neighborhood scale and lose the cross-density comparison.

**Q5. Show that k-NN distance is an implicit density estimator.**

*Answer.* In $\mathbb{R}^d$ with density $f$, the probability mass in a ball of radius $r$ around a point of density $f$ is approximately $f \cdot V_d r^d$, where $V_d$ is the unit-ball volume. If we sample $n$ points, the expected number inside such a ball is $n f V_d r^d$. Setting this to $k$ and solving: $r \approx (k/(n f V_d))^{1/d}$. Thus $s_k \propto (1/f)^{1/d}$ — large $s_k$ corresponds to small $f$. This is exactly the $k$-nearest-neighbor density estimator.

#### Applied

**Q6. You're building a fraud detector and user features are 500-dimensional. Would you apply LOF directly? If not, what would you do?**

*Answer.* Not directly. At 500 dims, Euclidean distances concentrate and LOF loses discriminative power. Options: (1) project to lower dimensions with PCA, a UMAP embedding, or a supervised embedding; (2) use a tree-based detector (Isolation Forest) that is less distance-dependent; (3) train an autoencoder and run LOF on its bottleneck. I'd probably try (2) as a strong baseline and (3) if I want localized sensitivity.

**Q7. You notice LOF scores of 1.1 for some true anomalies and 1.3 for some inliers. What went wrong?**

*Answer.* LOF scores are not globally comparable across very heterogeneous datasets; normalization is only approximate. Likely issues: (a) $k$ too large, smoothing out anomalies; (b) anomalies appear in small clusters of their own ("micro-clusters"), so their LOF looks low because neighbors within that cluster are similar; (c) inliers near cluster boundaries have genuinely elevated LOF because of edge effects. Remedies: lower $k$, sweep multiple $k$ and take the max, or treat LOF as a ranking rather than a threshold.

#### Debugging & failure-mode

**Q8. Your LOF implementation returns NaN for many points. Why?**

*Answer.* Duplicates in the data, causing $d_k = 0$ and lrd $= \infty$. When those infinities enter the averaging in LOF, you get $\infty/\infty$. Fix: de-duplicate, or add a tiny epsilon to all reach-dists, or use the "extended LOF" variants that handle ties.

**Q9. The k-NN distance detector flags an entire sparse cluster as anomalous. How would you re-architect?**

*Answer.* This is the canonical reason to switch to LOF — it explicitly normalizes for local density so a genuinely sparse-but-normal cluster will have LOF ≈ 1. Alternatively, one could use a density-based method like DBSCAN to discover clusters of varying density and flag only non-cluster points.

#### Probing follow-ups

**Q10. Is LOF a metric? Is reach-dist a metric?**

*Answer.* LOF is a score, not a metric. reach-dist is not a metric: it is asymmetric (reach-dist$(p,o) \ne$ reach-dist$(o,p)$ in general) and does not satisfy the triangle inequality. The authors were explicit that reach-dist is a smoothing device, not a geometric object.

**Q11. How do you set a threshold on LOF scores in production?**

*Answer.* Several approaches: (a) label a small validation set of anomalies and inliers, compute the LOF threshold that maximizes F1 or achieves a target precision; (b) use a quantile of the training LOF distribution (e.g., top 1%); (c) run LOF over a sweep of $k$ and calibrate per-$k$; (d) combine with other scores in an ensemble (see §6). In practice I would avoid raw thresholds and use rank-based alerting.

**Q12. Compare LOF to DBSCAN.**

*Answer.* DBSCAN is a clustering algorithm that implicitly labels points as "noise" if they lack $\ge$ minPts within $\epsilon$. It gives a hard normal/outlier label and is sensitive to the $\epsilon$ global parameter, which is problematic for variable-density data. LOF gives a continuous score, is local, and naturally handles mixed densities — but is more expensive per point and harder to threshold. DBSCAN is a clustering tool; LOF is a dedicated detector.

## 3. Density-Based Methods: Isolation Forest and One-Class SVM

### 3.1 Motivation & Intuition

Distance-based methods ask: *how far is this point from its neighbors?* Density-based methods ask the complementary question: *how likely is this point under a model of normality?* Two radically different instantiations dominate practice.

**Isolation Forest (iForest).** Instead of modeling where normal data *is*, it models where normal data *isn't* — or rather, how *hard* it is to isolate a point by random splits. Anomalies, being few and different, are easy to isolate with a few random cuts. Normal points, embedded deep inside dense regions, require many cuts. This "inverted" perspective makes iForest exceptionally scalable and robust in high dimensions.

*Concrete intuition.* Imagine playing "20 questions" with random yes/no splits. To pinpoint a single oddball in the corner of feature space, you need 2–3 random splits. To pinpoint one specific normal point in a dense crowd, you need 20–30. The depth of isolation is the anomaly score.

**One-class SVM (OC-SVM).** Learn a boundary around the training data in a (possibly kernel-induced) feature space; anything outside is anomalous. Under the hood, it's a kernelized quadratic program that finds a hyperplane separating the data from the origin with maximum margin. With an RBF kernel, this hyperplane becomes a curved envelope around the normal region.

**Real-world ML use cases.**

- **Isolation Forest:** de facto baseline at many companies for tabular, high-dimensional anomaly detection (logs, metrics, fraud). Scales to millions of rows and hundreds of features with low memory.
- **One-class SVM:** semi-supervised "novelty detection" when you have a clean training set of normals and want a principled decision function. Popular in smaller-data biomedical and security applications.

### 3.2 Conceptual Foundations

#### 3.2.1 Isolation Forest

**Key objects.**

- **Isolation tree (iTree).** A binary tree built by recursively picking a random feature and a random split value until each leaf contains one point (or until a depth limit).
- **Path length $h(x)$.** Number of edges from root to the leaf that isolates $x$. Short path ⇒ easy to isolate ⇒ suspect.
- **$c(n)$ — average path length in a BST of $n$ nodes.** A normalization factor so scores are comparable across trees and sample sizes: $c(n) = 2 H(n-1) - 2(n-1)/n$, where $H$ is the harmonic number.
- **Anomaly score $s(x, n) = 2^{-E[h(x)]/c(n)}$.** Close to 1 ⇒ anomaly, close to 0.5 ⇒ ambiguous, close to 0 ⇒ strongly normal.

**How it interacts.**

1. Subsample $\psi$ points (default 256) from the training data — small subsamples reduce "swamping" and "masking."
2. Build $t$ random trees independently, each to depth $\lceil \log_2 \psi \rceil$.
3. For each query point, average the path length across trees, normalize by $c(\psi)$, convert to $s$.

**Assumptions.**

- Anomalies are "few and different" (the paper's phrase) — they occupy sparsely populated regions.
- Random axis-aligned splits can meaningfully isolate them. Breaks down for anomalies defined by correlated features (where an oblique cut would be needed).

**What breaks.**

- **Correlated anomalies.** A point anomalous in the direction of feature $X + Y$ but normal in $X$ and $Y$ individually may require many axis-aligned splits. *Extended Isolation Forest* (Hariri et al., 2018) addresses this with random hyperplane splits.
- **Irrelevant features.** They dilute the random feature selection; many splits are wasted.
- **Categorical-heavy data.** Random split on a categorical with many levels is weird; needs careful encoding.

#### 3.2.2 One-Class SVM

**Key objects.**

- **Kernel $K(x, x')$.** Inner product in some (possibly infinite-dimensional) feature space $\phi$. RBF kernel $K(x,x') = \exp(-\gamma \|x - x'\|^2)$ is by far the most common.
- **Decision function.** $f(x) = \sum_i \alpha_i K(x_i, x) - \rho$. $f(x) \ge 0$ ⇒ normal; $f(x) < 0$ ⇒ anomaly.
- **$\nu$ (nu).** A parameter in $(0, 1]$ that is both an upper bound on the fraction of training points allowed outside the boundary and a lower bound on the fraction of support vectors.
- **$\gamma$ (for RBF).** Controls how tight the boundary fits around the training data.

**How it interacts.**

1. Choose a kernel and hyperparameters $\nu, \gamma$.
2. Solve a convex QP that finds the maximum-margin hyperplane separating $\phi(x)$ from the origin.
3. For each query point, evaluate $f(x)$. Negative ⇒ anomaly.

**Assumptions.**

- Training data is (mostly) clean — the boundary is fit to what you call "normal."
- Feature space via the chosen kernel makes the normal region approximately separable from the origin.
- You have enough data for the QP to generalize — OC-SVM tends to overfit on small datasets with a tight RBF.

**What breaks.**

- **Contaminated training data.** $\nu$ provides some tolerance, but heavy contamination biases the boundary outward.
- **Poor kernel choice.** Bad $\gamma$ produces a boundary that is either everything (useless) or only the training points themselves (overfit).
- **Scaling.** Solving the QP is $O(n^2)$ to $O(n^3)$, prohibitive on large data.

### 3.3 Mathematical Formulation

#### 3.3.1 Isolation Forest — why path length correlates with anomaly

Consider a sub-sample $S$ of size $\psi$ and build an iTree. A random split picks feature $j$ uniformly, then a split value $v$ uniformly in the range of that feature within the current node. Recurse.

**Key observation.** In any single node containing a point $x$ that is far from the rest, a random split value is likely to land *between* $x$ and the rest, isolating $x$ in one child. This is quantified as follows. If $x$ has value $v_x$ on feature $j$ and the other points in the node have values in $[a, b]$ with $v_x \notin [a, b]$, a random split in $[\min(a, v_x), \max(b, v_x)]$ isolates $x$ iff the split lies between $v_x$ and the nearest extreme of $[a, b]$. For an isolated point, this probability is non-trivially large; thus expected path length for anomalies is small.

**Normalization via BST average path length.** For a dataset of $\psi$ points, the expected path length in an unsuccessful search in a binary search tree is

$$
c(\psi) = 2 H(\psi - 1) - \frac{2(\psi - 1)}{\psi}, \qquad H(k) = \sum_{i=1}^k 1/i \approx \ln k + \gamma_{EM},
$$

where $\gamma_{EM} \approx 0.5772$ is Euler's constant. We use $c(\psi)$ to normalize path length, because the random iTree structure closely resembles a random BST.

**Score.**

$$
s(x, \psi) = 2^{-E[h(x)] / c(\psi)}.
$$

*Why $2^{-\cdot}$?* It maps $[0, \infty)$ to $(0, 1]$ with $s = 0.5$ when $E[h] = c(\psi)$ (the generic point). Short paths ($E[h] \to 0$) give $s \to 1$; long paths give $s \to 0$. Exponentiating by 2 matches the tree's binary branching, so the score is roughly linear in "how many levels above/below average."

**Why subsample to $\psi = 256$?**

- **Swamping.** In large samples, normal points near an anomaly's isolation boundary also get cut quickly — their path lengths shrink. Small $\psi$ reduces the crowd around anomalies.
- **Masking.** When multiple anomalies cluster, they can hide each other (each takes many cuts to isolate within the anomaly cluster). Subsampling thins them.

**Complexity.** Training: $O(t \psi \log \psi)$ — independent of $n$. Scoring: $O(t \log \psi)$ per point. This is extremely favorable.

#### 3.3.2 One-Class SVM — primal and dual derivation

**Primal problem (Schölkopf et al., 2001).**

$$
\min_{w, \rho, \xi} \quad \tfrac{1}{2}\|w\|^2 - \rho + \frac{1}{\nu n}\sum_{i=1}^n \xi_i
$$

$$
\text{s.t.} \quad \langle w, \phi(x_i) \rangle \ge \rho - \xi_i, \quad \xi_i \ge 0.
$$

*Reading this.* We seek a hyperplane $\{z : \langle w, z \rangle = \rho\}$ in the feature space such that **as much of the data as possible lies on the positive side** (i.e., away from the origin), with a large margin $\rho / \|w\|$. Slacks $\xi_i \ge 0$ allow some training points to fall on the wrong side; the penalty is $\frac{1}{\nu n}\sum \xi_i$.

*Why separate from the origin?* Because in many kernel spaces (notably RBF), the origin is a "generic" point not associated with any training example, and maximizing distance from it is a proxy for enclosing the training data tightly.

**Dual derivation (sketch).** Introduce Lagrange multipliers $\alpha_i \ge 0$ for the first constraints and $\beta_i \ge 0$ for $\xi_i \ge 0$. KKT:

$$
w = \sum_i \alpha_i \phi(x_i), \qquad \alpha_i + \beta_i = \frac{1}{\nu n}, \qquad \sum_i \alpha_i = 1.
$$

Substituting $w$ back yields the dual

$$
\min_\alpha \tfrac{1}{2} \sum_{i,j} \alpha_i \alpha_j K(x_i, x_j) \quad \text{s.t.} \quad 0 \le \alpha_i \le \tfrac{1}{\nu n}, \quad \sum_i \alpha_i = 1.
$$

This is a quadratic program solvable by SMO-style algorithms.

**Decision function.**

$$
f(x) = \sum_i \alpha_i K(x_i, x) - \rho, \qquad \text{anomaly iff } f(x) < 0.
$$

**The meaning of $\nu$.** A theorem of the original paper states:

- The fraction of training points with $\xi_i > 0$ (i.e., *outside* the boundary) is at most $\nu$.
- The fraction of support vectors ($\alpha_i > 0$) is at least $\nu$.

So $\nu$ is an upper bound on the training-time false-positive rate. If you expect ~1% contamination, set $\nu \approx 0.01$.

**Role of $\gamma$ (RBF).** $\gamma$ controls the width of each Gaussian: large $\gamma$ ⇒ narrow bumps ⇒ a boundary that hugs each training point (overfits). Small $\gamma$ ⇒ smooth, possibly globular boundary (underfits). Median-heuristic or cross-validation against a small labeled set is standard.

**Connection to support vector data description (SVDD).** SVDD fits the smallest ball enclosing the data in feature space; with an RBF kernel it's equivalent to OC-SVM up to a sign.

### 3.4 Worked Examples

#### Isolation Forest — by hand

**Dataset.** 2D points:

```
normals: (1,1), (1.1,0.9), (0.9,1.1), (1.2,1.05), (0.95,1.2), (1.05,0.95)
anomaly: (8, 8)
```

Build one iTree with $\psi = 7$ (the whole set).

- **Root.** Feature range: $x \in [0.9, 8]$. Pick feature $x$ randomly, split value uniformly in $[0.9, 8]$, say $v = 4.5$. Left child: all 6 normals ($x \le 4.5$). Right child: just $(8, 8)$. Right is a leaf.
- **Path length for (8,8) = 1.** One cut sufficed.
- **Left subtree.** 6 points in a tight cluster; random splits take ~2–3 levels to isolate each.
- **Average normal path length ~2.5. Anomaly path length = 1.**

Now build 100 trees; average path lengths stabilize. Normalize by $c(7) = 2 H(6) - 12/7 \approx 2(1+1/2+1/3+1/4+1/5+1/6) - 12/7 \approx 4.9 - 1.71 \approx 3.19$.

- $s((8,8)) = 2^{-1/3.19} \approx 2^{-0.314} \approx 0.80$. Strongly anomalous.
- $s((1,1)) = 2^{-2.5/3.19} \approx 2^{-0.78} \approx 0.58$. Borderline, but among normals it's the typical score; relative rank matters more than absolute value.

#### One-Class SVM — conceptual walkthrough

**Same dataset.** Fit OC-SVM with RBF kernel, $\nu = 0.1$, $\gamma = 1$.

- The QP finds $\alpha_i$ concentrated on the normals. The offset $\rho$ is set so that ≤ 10% of training points lie outside the boundary.
- The decision function $f(x)$ will be positive over a region roughly enclosing the normals, negative elsewhere.
- $f((8,8)) \ll 0$ — anomaly.
- $f((1,1)) > 0$ — normal.
- A boundary point like $(1.5, 1.5)$ might sit just inside or outside depending on $\gamma$: larger $\gamma$ (tighter) ⇒ outside.

The key experimental knob is the pair $(\nu, \gamma)$. $\nu$ controls target false positive rate; $\gamma$ controls shape tightness.

### 3.5 Relevance to Machine Learning Practice

**Isolation Forest** is typically the first method tried on tabular anomaly detection because:

- It scales: linear in $n$ after subsampling, and parallelizes trivially over trees.
- It's nearly hyperparameter-free (only $t$ and $\psi$, and both have sensible defaults).
- It's robust to irrelevant features, though correlated anomalies benefit from Extended iForest.
- Production-friendly: trees serialize compactly, scoring is fast.

Use it as a strong baseline for:

- Log anomaly detection.
- Feature drift detection on multivariate features.
- High-dimensional tabular fraud.
- Pre-filtering candidates for more expensive downstream models.

**One-Class SVM** shines when:

- Training data is clean (semi-supervised novelty detection).
- Data is low-to-moderate dimensional and modest in size (up to tens of thousands of points).
- You want a kernel-controlled decision boundary and are willing to tune $\gamma$.

Avoid OC-SVM on:

- Very large datasets (QP is $O(n^2)$).
- Data with heavy contamination (the boundary biases outward).
- Streaming settings (incremental OC-SVM variants exist but are uncommon).

**Trade-offs summary.**

| Aspect | Isolation Forest | One-Class SVM |
|---|---|---|
| Training cost | $O(t \psi \log \psi)$ | $O(n^2)$ to $O(n^3)$ |
| Scoring cost | $O(t \log \psi)$ | $O(n_{SV})$ per query |
| Robustness to contamination | High | Moderate (controlled by $\nu$) |
| Handles high dim | Yes | Degrades |
| Interpretability | Moderate (feature usage in splits) | Low (weighted kernel sum) |
| Typical best-use | Large tabular | Small clean semi-supervised |

### 3.6 Common Pitfalls & Misconceptions

**Isolation Forest:**

- **Treating the raw anomaly score as a probability.** It's normalized to $[0,1]$ but it is not calibrated. Convert to rank or calibrate on labeled data.
- **Setting $\psi$ too large.** Increases swamping/masking. 256 is a sensible default; sensitivity analysis is cheap.
- **Using iForest on highly correlated anomalies.** Axis-aligned splits struggle. Use Extended iForest or rotate the data (random projection).
- **Ignoring categorical encoding.** One-hot a categorical with 100 levels and iForest wastes splits on each one-hot axis. Consider target encoding or ordinal encoding for anomaly purposes.
- **Thinking path length is directly the score.** The exponential transform matters — it compresses the dynamic range and makes scores comparable across datasets.

**One-Class SVM:**

- **Not scaling features.** RBF kernel is distance-based; unscaled features dominate $\gamma$ choice.
- **Picking $\nu$ to tune accuracy rather than expected contamination.** $\nu$ is a probabilistic semantic parameter, not an accuracy knob.
- **Overfitting via large $\gamma$.** On small data, a tight RBF will memorize training points and label *anything else* anomalous.
- **Forgetting OC-SVM is semi-supervised.** If you feed it a dataset with 10% real anomalies mixed in, the boundary will include them as "normal" and fail at test time.

### 3.7 Interview Preparation — Density-Based

#### Foundational

**Q1. Explain the core idea of Isolation Forest in a minute.**

*Answer.* Anomalies are few and different, so a random axis-aligned split on data is disproportionately likely to isolate them quickly. iForest builds many random trees by recursive random splits and measures the expected depth at which each point becomes isolated. Short path = easy to isolate = anomaly. We average across trees, normalize by the expected path length in a random BST of the same size, and convert to a $(0,1]$ score via $2^{-E[h]/c(\psi)}$.

**Q2. What does $\nu$ control in OC-SVM?**

*Answer.* $\nu \in (0, 1]$ is both an upper bound on the fraction of training points that may lie outside the learned decision boundary (training-time false-positive rate) and a lower bound on the fraction of support vectors. In practice, set $\nu$ to your estimate of the training contamination level; if the data is perfectly clean, set it small (e.g., 0.01).

#### Mathematical

**Q3. Why is the Isolation Forest anomaly score $s = 2^{-E[h]/c(\psi)}$ and not just $E[h]$?**

*Answer.* Three reasons: (1) normalization — dividing by $c(\psi)$ makes scores comparable across sample sizes; (2) dynamic range — the exponential squashes path length to $(0, 1]$, giving a bounded score; (3) interpretability — $s = 0.5$ marks the "typical" point ($E[h] = c(\psi)$), $s \to 1$ strong anomaly, $s \to 0$ deep inlier.

**Q4. Derive the OC-SVM dual from the primal.**

*Answer.* Primal: $\min \tfrac{1}{2}\|w\|^2 - \rho + \tfrac{1}{\nu n}\sum \xi_i$ subject to $\langle w, \phi(x_i)\rangle \ge \rho - \xi_i$ and $\xi_i \ge 0$. Lagrangian:

$$
L = \tfrac12 \|w\|^2 - \rho + \tfrac{1}{\nu n}\sum \xi_i - \sum \alpha_i (\langle w, \phi_i\rangle - \rho + \xi_i) - \sum \beta_i \xi_i.
$$

Stationarity: $\partial L/\partial w = w - \sum \alpha_i \phi_i = 0$ ⇒ $w = \sum \alpha_i \phi_i$. $\partial L/\partial \rho = -1 + \sum \alpha_i = 0$ ⇒ $\sum \alpha_i = 1$. $\partial L/\partial \xi_i = \tfrac{1}{\nu n} - \alpha_i - \beta_i = 0$. Since $\beta_i \ge 0$, $\alpha_i \le \tfrac{1}{\nu n}$. Substituting $w$ back yields the dual:

$$
\min \tfrac12 \sum_{i,j} \alpha_i \alpha_j K(x_i, x_j) \quad \text{s.t.}\quad 0 \le \alpha_i \le \tfrac{1}{\nu n}, \ \sum \alpha_i = 1.
$$

The decision function follows from $f(x) = \langle w, \phi(x)\rangle - \rho = \sum \alpha_i K(x_i, x) - \rho$.

**Q5. Why does iForest normalize by $c(\psi) \approx 2 \ln \psi$?**

*Answer.* $c(\psi)$ is the expected path length in a random binary search tree with $\psi$ nodes, approximately $2(\ln(\psi-1) + \gamma_{EM}) - 2(\psi-1)/\psi$. An iTree is random but structurally similar, so $c(\psi)$ estimates the path length for a "generic" point. Dividing by it makes $E[h]/c(\psi)$ a dimensionless ratio with value near 1 for generic points, close to 0 for anomalies.

#### Applied

**Q6. You're choosing between Isolation Forest and OC-SVM for a production fraud system with 10M transactions and 200 features. Which and why?**

*Answer.* Isolation Forest, clearly. OC-SVM's QP scales quadratically to cubically in $n$ and will not complete on 10M rows. iForest trains in seconds (subsample to 256 per tree, 100 trees), scores in microseconds per point, and tolerates modest contamination. I'd reserve OC-SVM for a smaller offline validation set or as a complementary method in a feature-bagged ensemble (§6) on lower-dimensional projections.

**Q7. You deploy an OC-SVM and test-time false-positive rate is 40%, not the 1% you set via $\nu$. What happened?**

*Answer.* Three common causes: (1) **distribution shift** — test data isn't drawn from the same distribution as training. $\nu$ bounds *training* FP rate, not production. (2) **$\gamma$ too large** — boundary hugs training points tightly; even slight perturbations at inference appear anomalous. (3) **Feature scaling drift** — standardization parameters are stale. Fix: retrain with fresh data, cross-validate $\gamma$, and enforce a calibration step (Platt scaling or isotonic) against a labeled validation set.

#### Debugging & failure-mode

**Q8. iForest flags normal points as anomalous and misses obvious anomalies. What do you check?**

*Answer.* Checklist: (a) Did you pre-process features reasonably? iForest is robust but extreme scaling can cause degenerate splits. (b) Are the anomalies defined by *feature combinations* rather than single features? Try Extended iForest. (c) Is $\psi$ so large that swamping/masking dominates? Lower to 64 or 128. (d) Are categorical variables one-hotted with very high cardinality, creating many useless split candidates? Re-encode. (e) Check per-feature importance (how often each feature is split near the root) — if one feature dominates, the forest is essentially univariate.

**Q9. OC-SVM returns `f(x) = 0` for every test point. What's wrong?**

*Answer.* Almost certainly numerical — the kernel matrix collapsed (very small $\gamma$ causing nearly constant kernel values, or very large $\gamma$ causing off-diagonal zeros). Standardize features, try a median-heuristic $\gamma$ (set $\gamma = 1 / (2 \cdot \text{median squared pairwise distance})$), and check that `decision_function` output has dynamic range.

#### Probing follow-ups

**Q10. iForest's splits are axis-aligned. What kind of anomalies does this miss?**

*Answer.* Anomalies defined by correlated feature combinations. Example: two features whose normal region is a thin diagonal band; a point off the diagonal (but within each feature's marginal range) is anomalous. Axis-aligned splits need many levels to isolate it. Extended iForest (Hariri, Kind, Brunner) uses random hyperplane splits and fixes this.

**Q11. Why doesn't iForest need a contamination parameter at train time, while OC-SVM does?**

*Answer.* iForest is unsupervised: it builds trees on the data as-is and assigns anomaly scores. The "contamination" parameter in sklearn is *only* used to pick a threshold on the continuous score — it doesn't affect the model. OC-SVM, being a boundary-fitting method, literally uses $\nu$ inside the optimization to decide how much training data to exclude. iForest's robustness to contamination comes from the subsampling + randomness structure, not from an explicit parameter.

**Q12. In a deep-learning world, why are iForests still widely used?**

*Answer.* Because they're cheap, interpretable-ish, train in seconds on laptops, need almost no tuning, and produce competitive results on tabular data — where deep methods often don't dominate. For structured enterprise data, iForest + good feature engineering + a calibration layer is hard to beat operationally.

## 4. Reconstruction-Based Methods: PCA and Autoencoders

### 4.1 Motivation & Intuition

If normal data lives on a low-dimensional structure — a subspace, a manifold, a typical trajectory — then a model that compresses and reconstructs typical data will do so with low error. Anomalies, lying off this structure, will be reconstructed poorly. **Reconstruction error becomes an anomaly score.**

**Why this works.** Real data is almost never "full-dimensional." A 1000-pixel face image lives on a manifold of maybe 50 dimensions (pose, lighting, expression, identity). A 50-dimensional user feature vector might live on a 10-dimensional subspace reflecting genuine behavior patterns. A model that learns this low-dim structure **cannot represent** out-of-distribution points without distortion — and that distortion is the signal.

**Real ML use cases.**

- **Computer vision anomaly detection.** Autoencoders trained on normal product images flag defective units by reconstruction quality.
- **Network intrusion detection.** Autoencoders on connection-feature vectors flag unusual traffic patterns.
- **Medical imaging.** Reconstruction-based detection of pathologies in MRI/CT scans.
- **Sensor data in IoT.** PCA residuals from typical sensor correlations flag equipment faults.
- **Text / log anomalies.** Autoencoders on embeddings of log lines flag unusual messages.

### 4.2 Conceptual Foundations

**Common framework.** Train an encoder $f: \mathbb{R}^d \to \mathbb{R}^k$ (with $k < d$) and a decoder $g: \mathbb{R}^k \to \mathbb{R}^d$ such that $g(f(x)) \approx x$ on normal data. For a new point $x$, the **reconstruction error**

$$
\mathcal{R}(x) = \| x - g(f(x)) \|^2
$$

is the anomaly score.

**PCA.** Linear encoder $f(x) = U^\top (x - \mu)$ where $U$ is the matrix of top-$k$ eigenvectors of the data covariance. Decoder $g(z) = \mu + U z$. The reconstruction is the orthogonal projection onto the $k$-dimensional subspace spanned by principal components.

**Autoencoder.** Neural network with an information bottleneck. Encoder and decoder are arbitrary nonlinear functions (typically feed-forward nets or convolutional nets for images), trained to minimize $\mathcal{R}$ on normal data.

**Assumptions.**

- Normal data lies near a low-dimensional structure (linear subspace for PCA, nonlinear manifold for autoencoders).
- Training data is predominantly normal.
- Anomalies lie *off* that structure — they should not be easily reconstructable by interpolating normals.
- Reconstruction error is a monotonic function of "anomalousness."

**What breaks.**

- **Anomalies inside the learned manifold.** Out-of-distribution points whose projection onto the manifold is a plausible normal point will have low reconstruction error and be missed. Example: a fake face generated by a GAN trained on real faces — it may lie on the face manifold.
- **Too much capacity.** An autoencoder with no bottleneck (enough capacity to memorize) will reconstruct anomalies too. Regularization (small bottleneck, denoising, contractive, variational) is essential.
- **Training with contamination.** The model learns to reconstruct anomalies too, blunting their signal.
- **Feature scaling.** PCA is covariance-based; unscaled features dominate.

### 4.3 Mathematical Formulation

#### 4.3.1 PCA reconstruction

Given data matrix $X \in \mathbb{R}^{n \times d}$, center it: $\tilde X = X - \mathbf{1}\mu^\top$. Compute covariance $\Sigma = \tilde X^\top \tilde X / (n-1)$. Eigendecomposition $\Sigma = V \Lambda V^\top$ with eigenvalues $\lambda_1 \ge \dots \ge \lambda_d \ge 0$.

Take $U = V_{:, 1:k}$ — the top $k$ eigenvectors. The PCA projection is $Z = \tilde X U$, reconstruction $\hat X = \mathbf{1}\mu^\top + Z U^\top$.

**Reconstruction error of $x$** (relative to center):

$$
\mathcal{R}(x) = \|(x - \mu) - U U^\top (x - \mu)\|^2 = \| (I - U U^\top)(x - \mu) \|^2 = \sum_{j=k+1}^d \langle v_j, x - \mu \rangle^2.
$$

**The residual subspace interpretation.** $I - UU^\top$ projects onto the $(d-k)$-dimensional subspace orthogonal to the top components. The reconstruction error is the squared norm of $x - \mu$ in that residual subspace. Low-variance directions (small $\lambda_j$) are where normal data barely varies — hence any variance there is suspicious.

**Hotelling's $T^2$ vs $Q$ statistic.**

- **Hotelling's $T^2$** (score distance): $T^2 = z^\top \Lambda_k^{-1} z$ — how unusual $x$ is *within* the principal subspace. Picks up anomalies that are extreme along principal directions.
- **$Q$ statistic** (reconstruction error): $\mathcal{R}(x)$ above — how unusual $x$ is *orthogonal to* the principal subspace. Picks up anomalies that violate the learned correlations.

In practice, monitor both. A classical process control pattern.

**Why off-manifold = anomalous.** If the normal process produces $x \approx \mu + Uz + \epsilon$ with small noise $\epsilon$ in the residual subspace, then normal $\mathcal{R}(x) \approx \|\epsilon_\perp\|^2$, bounded. An anomaly breaking the correlation structure adds signal to the residual subspace, inflating $\mathcal{R}$.

#### 4.3.2 Autoencoder

**Architecture.** $f_\theta: \mathbb{R}^d \to \mathbb{R}^k$, $g_\phi: \mathbb{R}^k \to \mathbb{R}^d$ parameterized by neural networks. Training minimizes

$$
\mathcal{L}(\theta, \phi) = \frac{1}{n}\sum_i \|x_i - g_\phi(f_\theta(x_i))\|^2 \quad (\text{+ regularizers}).
$$

**Relation to PCA.** A linear autoencoder $f(x) = W_1 x$, $g(z) = W_2 z$ with squared-error loss and a $k$-dimensional bottleneck recovers (up to rotation) the PCA subspace. This is Bourlard & Kamp (1988).

**Regularization variants.**

- **Denoising AE:** input is corrupted $\tilde x = x + \epsilon$; target is $x$. Encourages learning a robust manifold rather than the identity.
- **Contractive AE:** add penalty $\lambda \|J_f(x)\|_F^2$ where $J_f$ is the Jacobian of the encoder. Forces local insensitivity, stabilizing the manifold.
- **Variational AE (VAE):** probabilistic latent $z \sim q_\phi(z | x)$; training maximizes the ELBO $\mathbb{E}_q[\log p_\phi(x | z)] - \text{KL}(q_\phi(z|x) \| p(z))$. The marginal likelihood $\log p(x)$ can be used as a more principled anomaly score.

**Scoring.**

- Simple: per-sample reconstruction error (MSE or L1).
- Per-feature: $|x_j - \hat x_j|$ to localize which features are off.
- Probabilistic (VAE): reconstruction likelihood or free energy.

**Why a bottleneck matters.** Without dimensionality restriction, the network can learn the identity mapping and reconstruct everything, including anomalies. The bottleneck forces it to prioritize directions where the training data actually varies — the "manifold."

### 4.4 Worked Examples

#### PCA on toy data

**Data.** Normal points on the line $y = x$ in 2D: $(0,0), (1,1), (2,2), (3,3)$. Anomaly: $(2, 0)$, clearly off the line.

- Mean: $(1.5, 1.5)$. Centered: $(-1.5, -1.5), (-0.5, -0.5), (0.5, 0.5), (1.5, 1.5)$.
- Covariance $\Sigma = \begin{pmatrix} 1.67 & 1.67 \\ 1.67 & 1.67 \end{pmatrix}$.
- Eigenvalues: $\lambda_1 = 3.33$, $\lambda_2 = 0$. Eigenvectors: $v_1 = (1,1)/\sqrt{2}$, $v_2 = (1,-1)/\sqrt{2}$.
- $k = 1$: keep $v_1$. Reconstruction projects onto the $y=x$ line.
- Anomaly $(2, 0)$ centered: $(0.5, -1.5)$. Projection onto $v_1$: $((0.5 - 1.5)/\sqrt{2}) \cdot (1,1)/\sqrt{2} = -0.5 \cdot (1,1)/2 = (-0.5, -0.5)$. Residual: $(0.5, -1.5) - (-0.5, -0.5) = (1.0, -1.0)$. $\mathcal{R} = 1^2 + 1^2 = 2.0$.
- Normal point $(1, 1)$ centered: $(-0.5, -0.5)$. Projection onto $v_1$: full vector. Residual: $(0,0)$. $\mathcal{R} = 0$.
- Clear separation.

#### Autoencoder conceptual example

Imagine a dataset of handwritten 7s. Train an autoencoder with a 16-dim bottleneck on 10,000 images of 7s.

- A new 7: encoder extracts (loop, stroke weight, slant, ...) into 16 numbers; decoder reconstructs a blurry but recognizable 7. MSE ≈ 0.002.
- A new 3: the encoder's 16 numbers cannot represent a 3; decoder produces something between a 7 and a 3. MSE ≈ 0.05 — 25× the normal error.
- A noisy 7: MSE slightly elevated (maybe 0.005) — detectable but moderate.
- A constant gray image: the decoder tries to produce a 7; MSE very high. Detected.

### 4.5 Relevance to Machine Learning Practice

**PCA is appropriate when:**

- Data structure is approximately linear (or has been linearized by a careful transformation).
- Speed and interpretability matter — principal loadings can be examined.
- Small-to-medium datasets; PCA scales as $O(\min(nd^2, n^2 d))$.
- Process monitoring with $T^2$ and $Q$ statistics is a standard industrial practice.

**Autoencoders are appropriate when:**

- Data is high-dimensional and nonlinear (images, text, speech, complex tabular).
- You have enough normal data to train without overfitting.
- You're willing to spend compute and tuning time.
- Downstream systems can use the learned latent space for other tasks.

**When not to use reconstruction methods.**

- Anomalies that lie *on* the manifold — adversarial examples, forged-but-plausible data.
- Small datasets (autoencoders overfit; PCA could work if dimensionality is high).
- When you need well-calibrated probabilities (reconstruction error is not a probability without additional modeling, e.g., VAE likelihood).

**Trade-offs.**

- **Bias–variance.** Small bottleneck = high bias (may miss subtle anomalies on the manifold); large bottleneck = high variance (may reconstruct anomalies too well, missing them).
- **Interpretability.** PCA: high (loadings, explained variance). AE: low by default; saliency maps and per-feature reconstruction help.
- **Compute.** PCA: seconds. AE: hours of training, especially deep.
- **Contamination robustness.** Low by default; robust variants (robust PCA, denoising AE trained on noisy inliers) help.

### 4.6 Common Pitfalls & Misconceptions

- **Not standardizing features before PCA.** PCA operates on variance; unscaled features dominate.
- **Picking $k$ by a fixed "90% variance" rule without validation.** For anomaly detection, the residual subspace (the *last* components) is what carries the signal, so losing it matters. Pick $k$ by held-out reconstruction on labeled anomalies if possible.
- **Using a wide autoencoder without a bottleneck.** It'll learn the identity. Add a tight bottleneck, denoising noise, or contractive regularization.
- **Training on contaminated data without awareness.** The AE learns to reconstruct anomalies; their scores converge toward normals'. Robust training (trimming high-loss points during training, or Robust PCA's L1 formulation) helps.
- **Treating MSE as a calibrated probability.** It isn't. Calibrate against a validation set, or use a probabilistic model (VAE).
- **Assuming lower $k$ is always better for anomaly detection.** Too small $k$ makes normal points reconstruct badly too, destroying discrimination.
- **Ignoring that AE may be *too good* a nonlinear universal approximator.** Given enough capacity and training, it reconstructs anything. Regularize carefully.
- **Not checking per-feature errors.** Global MSE can hide per-feature insight. In multivariate IoT, one sensor going off is the signal; its per-feature error dominates but gets averaged out in MSE. Use max per-feature error or feature-wise Z-scores of residuals.

### 4.7 Interview Preparation — Reconstruction-Based

#### Foundational

**Q1. Why does a low reconstruction error imply a point is "normal"?**

*Answer.* We fit the model on normal data, so it learns a compact representation of the normal manifold. Normal points project cleanly onto that manifold and are reconstructed with small error. Anomalies, off the manifold, cannot be represented by the low-dim latent; the decoder produces a close-but-wrong approximation, leaving residual error. The logic assumes: (a) training data is normal, (b) the model is low-capacity enough that it cannot represent anomalies. Both must be verified.

**Q2. What's the relationship between PCA and a linear autoencoder?**

*Answer.* A linear AE with squared-error loss, $k$-dim bottleneck, and no nonlinearities learns a subspace that spans the top-$k$ PCA directions (up to rotation within the subspace). Proof: the minimizer of $\mathbb{E}[\|x - W_2 W_1 x\|^2]$ subject to rank $k$ is exactly PCA by the Eckart-Young theorem. The distinction is that PCA gives orthogonal components directly; a linear AE finds *any* rank-$k$ factorization of the same subspace.

#### Mathematical

**Q3. Derive the PCA reconstruction error formula.**

*Answer.* Let $x \in \mathbb{R}^d$ centered, $U \in \mathbb{R}^{d \times k}$ with orthonormal columns spanning the top-$k$ subspace. The optimal $k$-dim representation minimizing $\|x - Uz\|^2$ is $z^* = U^\top x$ (from $\partial/\partial z = -2U^\top(x - Uz) = 0$). Reconstruction: $\hat x = U U^\top x$. Residual: $x - \hat x = (I - UU^\top)x$. Note $(I - UU^\top)^2 = I - UU^\top$ (projection). Error: $\|x - \hat x\|^2 = x^\top (I - UU^\top) x = \sum_{j=k+1}^d \langle v_j, x\rangle^2$ where $v_j$ are eigenvectors. This equals the variance of $x$ in the residual subspace.

**Q4. Why are VAE reconstruction probabilities better anomaly scores than deterministic AE MSE?**

*Answer.* Three reasons. (1) **Calibration:** VAE marginal likelihood $p(x) = \int p(x|z) p(z) dz$ (approximated via ELBO) is a true probability density. MSE is a scale-dependent number. (2) **Uncertainty:** VAE accounts for latent uncertainty — if multiple plausible latents reconstruct $x$, the likelihood integrates over them. (3) **Model fit:** training by maximum likelihood explicitly fits a density, aligned with what anomaly detection wants. Caveats: VAEs can assign surprisingly high likelihood to certain OOD data (Nalisnick et al.), so naive likelihood isn't a silver bullet; alternatives include likelihood ratios and typicality tests.

**Q5. Why does a non-zero reconstruction error for normal points exist at all?**

*Answer.* Because $k < d$. Even on pure normal data, the residual subspace captures low-variance noise in the data. The null distribution of $\mathcal{R}$ on normal data is non-trivial; we set thresholds relative to it (e.g., the 99th percentile of reconstruction error on a validation set of normals).

#### Applied

**Q6. You train an autoencoder on 1M images of normal product items. At inference, the reconstruction error is high for everything, including clean normal items. What's wrong?**

*Answer.* Many possible issues. Check in order: (1) input normalization mismatch between train and inference; (2) insufficient training — the model hasn't converged; (3) bottleneck too tight, so even normals reconstruct poorly; (4) domain shift — lighting/camera changed between training and production; (5) data augmentation used at train but not at inference. Diagnose by plotting training and validation loss curves and comparing error distributions on a known-clean sample.

**Q7. How would you choose the latent dimension $k$?**

*Answer.* (a) If some labeled anomalies are available, sweep $k$ and pick the value maximizing AUROC on validation. (b) Otherwise, use the **scree plot** of PCA eigenvalues to pick an elbow — but verify that anomaly detection quality is stable across neighboring values. (c) For autoencoders, start smaller than intuition suggests; an overly large $k$ allows anomaly reconstruction. (d) Consider an ensemble of several $k$ values (§6).

**Q8. Your autoencoder detects image anomalies well in lab data but misses real production defects. Why?**

*Answer.* Three common reasons: (1) the production defects are *on* the training manifold — small defects in plausible locations get reconstructed fine. Use more aggressive bottleneck or explicit defect augmentation. (2) Training data was too homogeneous; the real variation at inference includes normal variations the model hasn't seen, inflating baseline error and masking anomalies. (3) Contamination: training data included unlabeled defects, teaching the model to reconstruct them. Fix: curate a clean training set, or use a robust loss function (L1, Huber) that down-weights contaminated points during training.

#### Debugging & failure-mode

**Q9. PCA flags almost no anomalies. Why?**

*Answer.* Likely $k$ was chosen too high (say 95% variance), leaving almost nothing in the residual. Anomaly signal lives in the discarded components. Either lower $k$, or use both $T^2$ and $Q$ statistics (monitor the principal subspace and the residual subspace jointly).

**Q10. An autoencoder perfectly reconstructs even adversarial inputs. What went wrong structurally?**

*Answer.* Likely no bottleneck (or the bottleneck is wider than the intrinsic data dimensionality), or the model is so flexible it learned a near-identity function. Fix: reduce bottleneck, add denoising noise, add contractive penalty, or use a variational AE. Adversarial inputs are specifically designed to stay near the manifold, so even a proper AE may miss them — complement with input-uncertainty methods.

#### Probing follow-ups

**Q11. What is Robust PCA and when would you use it?**

*Answer.* Robust PCA (Candès et al., 2011) decomposes $M = L + S$ where $L$ is low-rank (the principal subspace) and $S$ is sparse (the outliers). Formally, solve $\min \|L\|_* + \lambda \|S\|_1$ where $\|\cdot\|_*$ is the nuclear norm. The $S$ matrix explicitly *is* the anomaly signal. Use it when training data is contaminated and you want the PCA fit to ignore outliers rather than letting them inflate the principal variance.

**Q12. You have time-series data. Would you use a vanilla AE?**

*Answer.* Vanilla AE treats each timestamp independently. Better options: recurrent autoencoders (LSTM encoder + LSTM decoder), 1D CNN autoencoders, transformer-based sequence models, or forecast-based detectors where the residual between predicted and observed is the score. Even better for structured time series: STL decomposition + residual-based detection (§7). The choice depends on whether anomalies are pointwise, contextual, or collective.

**Q13. Compare reconstruction-based and density-based detectors.**

*Answer.* Density-based detectors (iForest, OC-SVM) give a geometric score — "how unusual given data density." Reconstruction detectors give a projection-based score — "how well does the learned normal model represent this." They capture overlapping but distinct notions: a point that is locally rare *and* unreconstructable is clearly anomalous; a point rare but on the manifold will be caught only by density; a point dense but off the manifold (unusual for reconstruction models to produce) will be caught only by reconstruction. Ensembling both (§6) is common and effective.

## 5. Ensemble Methods: Feature Bagging and Score Combination

### 5.1 Motivation & Intuition

A single anomaly detector is a single opinion. Like classification, anomaly detection benefits from **ensembles** — combining many detectors to reduce variance, hedge against method-specific failure modes, and capture anomalies of different types.

**Why this matters specifically for anomaly detection.**

- No ground truth at training time. You can't do a validation-set tournament to pick the "best" detector.
- Different anomaly types (point vs contextual, global vs local, univariate vs multivariate) favor different detectors.
- High-dimensional data exposes each detector to different weaknesses; averaging cancels out idiosyncratic errors.

**Two orthogonal forms of ensembling.**

1. **Feature bagging.** Run many detectors on random subsets of features. Each detector sees a reduced view; aggregation combines their scores. Mirrors Random Forest's philosophy.
2. **Score combination.** Run multiple *different* detectors (iForest, LOF, OC-SVM, AE) on the same data and combine their scores. Heterogeneous ensemble.

**Real-world ML use cases.**

- Financial fraud detection platforms often layer iForest + k-NN + LOF + AE.
- Production monitoring systems run multiple drift detectors per feature (Z-score + KS test + isolation).
- Security/IDS systems use feature-bagged ensembles over different feature groups (packet headers, flow stats, content).

### 5.2 Conceptual Foundations

**Feature bagging (Lazarevic & Kumar, 2005).**

- Sample $m$ feature subsets $F_1, \dots, F_m$, each of size between $\lfloor d/2 \rfloor$ and $d-1$.
- Train the same base detector on each subspace.
- Combine per-point scores across detectors.

**Score combination approaches.**

- **Averaging.** Mean of normalized scores across detectors.
- **Max.** Take the maximum; any detector's flag counts.
- **Rank combination.** Convert each score to a percentile rank, then average ranks.
- **Weighted by diversity.** Up-weight detectors whose scores are uncorrelated with others.
- **Meta-model.** If labeled anomalies exist, train a logistic regression or gradient-boosted model on detector scores.

**Required preprocessing.** Anomaly scores across detectors live on wildly different scales. Always normalize before combining:

- **Z-score / standardization** of scores (works if scores are roughly Gaussian).
- **Gaussian tail probability**: $p(x) = \Phi((\text{score} - \mu)/\sigma)$.
- **Unification via ranks**: percentile in the sample.
- **Probabilistic calibration** (Gao & Tan, 2006): fit a two-component mixture to the score distribution and convert to posterior probability of anomaly.

**Assumptions.**

- Base detectors have non-trivially diverse errors. If they all fail in the same way, ensembling helps nothing.
- Normalization preserves ordering of anomalousness.
- Enough data to fit each detector reliably.

**What breaks.**

- **Correlated detectors.** Five iForests with the same hyperparameters on the same features just reduce tree-level variance, not method-level bias. Diversity matters.
- **Dominant detectors.** Without normalization, one detector's score range swamps all others in averaging.
- **Feature bagging with low-dimensional data.** If $d = 5$, random subspaces lose too much signal.

### 5.3 Mathematical Formulation

#### 5.3.1 Feature bagging — generic procedure

Let $s_f(x)$ denote the anomaly score from detector $f$. Draw feature subsets $F_1, \dots, F_m$, train detectors $f_1, \dots, f_m$, and combine:

- **Mean:** $\bar s(x) = \frac{1}{m}\sum_j s_{f_j}(x_{F_j})$
- **Max:** $s^*(x) = \max_j s_{f_j}(x_{F_j})$
- **Breadth-first (Lazarevic–Kumar):** rank points within each detector, aggregate ranks.

**Variance analysis.** If detector scores for an anomaly have mean $\mu_a$, variance $\sigma^2$, and pairwise correlation $\rho$, the variance of their mean is

$$
\text{Var}(\bar s) = \frac{\sigma^2}{m} + \frac{m-1}{m} \rho \sigma^2 \xrightarrow{m \to \infty} \rho \sigma^2.
$$

Variance reduces with more detectors but is floored by correlation $\rho$. This is identical to the Random Forest intuition: ensemble gain is bounded by diversity.

#### 5.3.2 Score normalization

**Gaussian scaling.** For a vector of scores $s_1, \dots, s_n$ from one detector on $n$ points, compute sample $\hat\mu, \hat\sigma$ (robustly), and transform to

$$
\tilde s_i = \frac{s_i - \hat\mu}{\hat\sigma}.
$$

**Gaussian tail probability.** Convert to $p_i = 1 - \Phi(\tilde s_i)$ — an estimated probability that a typical point is at least this extreme. Works well if the null score distribution is approximately Gaussian; otherwise biased.

**Rank normalization.**

$$
r_i = \frac{\text{rank}(s_i) - 1}{n - 1} \in [0, 1].
$$

Distribution-free; preserves ordering; always on $[0,1]$. Popular default.

**Mixture-model calibration (Gao–Tan).** Fit $p(s) = \pi_0 p_0(s) + \pi_1 p_1(s)$ where $p_0$ is the normal-score density and $p_1$ is the anomaly density. Posterior probability of anomaly:

$$
P(\text{anomaly} | s) = \frac{\pi_1 p_1(s)}{\pi_0 p_0(s) + \pi_1 p_1(s)}.
$$

Gives calibrated per-point probabilities but requires fitting a mixture, which can be unstable.

#### 5.3.3 Combining across heterogeneous detectors

Let $\tilde s_i^{(k)}$ be the normalized score from detector $k$ on point $i$. Common combiners:

- **Average:** $\bar s_i = \frac{1}{K} \sum_k \tilde s_i^{(k)}$ — stable, reduces variance, may under-emphasize rare agreements.
- **Max:** $\max_k \tilde s_i^{(k)}$ — fires if any detector is confident; good for diverse anomaly types, higher false-positive rate.
- **Geometric mean / product:** emphasizes agreement; all detectors must be mildly positive for a point to rank high.
- **Trimmed mean:** drop extremes before averaging; robust to detector failure.
- **Learned combiner:** if labeled anomalies exist, train a stacker (logistic regression, GBM). This requires labels and is most powerful; but anomaly labels are usually the scarce resource.

#### 5.3.4 A small theoretical result on max-combination

If each detector's false-positive rate on a typical normal point is $\alpha$ independently, the max-combined detector's FPR at the same threshold is roughly $1 - (1-\alpha)^K \approx K \alpha$ for small $\alpha$. So max is *aggressive*. Averaging, by contrast, reduces FPR under independence (by CLT-style concentration) at the cost of sensitivity.

Rule of thumb: **use max when recall is paramount; use average when precision matters; use rank-average as a safe default.**

### 5.4 Worked Example

**Setup.** 5 detectors trained on the same dataset:

- Z-score (Gaussian).
- IQR.
- Isolation Forest.
- LOF ($k=20$).
- Autoencoder reconstruction MSE.

**Raw scores for three points (point 1 = known anomaly; points 2 and 3 = normal):**

| Detector | Range | Point 1 | Point 2 | Point 3 |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Z-score | [0, 10] | 4.5 | 0.8 | 1.2 |
| IQR flag | {0, 1} | 1 | 0 | 0 |
| iForest | [0, 1] | 0.85 | 0.42 | 0.51 |
| LOF | [1, ∞) | 3.2 | 1.1 | 0.95 |
| AE MSE | [0, ∞) | 0.08 | 0.001 | 0.002 |

Raw averaging is nonsense (MSE gets rounded to zero). Normalize.

**Rank normalization** (assume these three are the only points, ranks 1=lowest):

| Detector | Point 1 rank | Point 2 rank | Point 3 rank |
|---|---|---|---|
| Z-score | 3 | 1 | 2 |
| IQR | 3 (tied with 0s broken randomly) | 1.5 | 1.5 |
| iForest | 3 | 1 | 2 |
| LOF | 3 | 2 | 1 |
| AE | 3 | 1 | 2 |

Rank averages: Point 1: $(3+3+3+3+3)/5 = 3$. Point 2: $(1+1.5+1+2+1)/5 = 1.3$. Point 3: $(2+1.5+2+1+2)/5 = 1.7$. 

Point 1 has the highest aggregated anomaly rank — correctly identified unanimously. Point 3 is slightly more suspicious than Point 2, consistent with weak signal in some detectors.

**Max combination.** If we'd used max, Point 1 would still win (all detectors agree). But if, say, only LOF had flagged a contextual anomaly that other detectors missed, max would catch it while averaging wouldn't.

**Feature bagging example.** Suppose $d = 50$. Train 50 iForests, each on a random 25-feature subset. For each point, average the 50 score outputs. A point that's anomalous only in a specific feature subset will receive a high score from those detectors whose subsets include it, and a low score otherwise. Averaging still places it above median — detection works, though max might be sharper here.

### 5.5 Relevance to Machine Learning Practice

**When to ensemble.**

- Production systems where a single detector has known blind spots.
- Heterogeneous anomaly types (point, contextual, collective).
- High-dimensional data where feature bagging buys robustness.
- When diverse detectors are cheap to run.

**When not to ensemble.**

- Low-data regime where each detector is unreliable.
- Strict latency budgets that preclude running multiple detectors.
- When detectors are near-identical (no diversity ⇒ no gain).

**Common patterns in practice.**

- iForest + LOF + AE: covers density, locality, and manifold.
- Per-feature Z-score + multivariate iForest: covers univariate and multivariate.
- Feature-bagged iForest (already implicit in iForest's random feature selection, but can be amplified).

**Trade-offs.**

- **Bias–variance.** Averaging reduces variance; bias persists if all detectors share a misspecification.
- **Interpretability.** Drops — you now have to explain a combined score. Mitigate by reporting top-contributing detectors per flagged point.
- **Compute.** Linear in number of detectors at both training and inference.
- **Maintenance.** More detectors ⇒ more things to update, recalibrate, and monitor.

### 5.6 Common Pitfalls & Misconceptions

- **Skipping normalization.** The single most common bug. Raw score averaging is dominated by the detector with the widest scale.
- **Averaging highly correlated detectors.** Zero marginal gain. Check pairwise correlations of detector outputs and prune redundant ones.
- **Using max when you care about precision.** Max is sensitivity-biased; FPR compounds across detectors.
- **Ensembling only within one family.** 10 iForests is not as diverse as iForest + LOF + AE.
- **Forgetting to retrain the ensemble when underlying data shifts.** Each detector ages at its own rate; recalibration cycles differ.
- **Treating the ensemble's score as "calibrated" without explicit calibration.** Post-hoc calibration (Platt, isotonic) against a labeled set is still needed.
- **Feature bagging on tiny feature sets.** With $d \le 10$, random subsetting discards too much signal.

### 5.7 Interview Preparation — Ensemble Methods

#### Foundational

**Q1. Why ensemble anomaly detectors at all?**

*Answer.* Different detectors capture different anomaly structures: density methods see globally rare points; LOF sees local anomalies; reconstruction methods see manifold violations. Ensembling (a) reduces variance across random splits and random seeds; (b) hedges against method-specific failure modes; (c) enables a richer notion of "anomaly" covering multiple types simultaneously. Without labels at training time, ensembling is one of the few tools for reliability.

**Q2. What's the difference between feature bagging and model stacking?**

*Answer.* Feature bagging: same base learner, different feature subsets, unsupervised combination (usually average or max). Stacking: different base learners, full feature set, combined by a *learned* meta-model — requires labels. In anomaly detection, feature bagging is the far more common because labels are scarce.

#### Mathematical

**Q3. Derive the variance of an average of $m$ correlated detectors.**

*Answer.* Let $\tilde s_1, \dots, \tilde s_m$ have equal variance $\sigma^2$ and pairwise correlation $\rho$. Then

$$
\text{Var}\left(\frac{1}{m}\sum_j \tilde s_j\right) = \frac{1}{m^2}\left(m \sigma^2 + m(m-1)\rho\sigma^2\right) = \frac{\sigma^2}{m} + \frac{m-1}{m}\rho\sigma^2.
$$

As $m \to \infty$, this tends to $\rho\sigma^2$ — the floor set by correlation. This is why diversity (small $\rho$) is more important than quantity (large $m$).

**Q4. Give one concrete normalization method and explain when it fails.**

*Answer.* Rank normalization: $\tilde s_i = \text{rank}(s_i) / n$. Advantages: distribution-free, bounded, preserves ordering, scale-invariant. Failure modes: (1) loses information about *how extreme* an anomaly is — the top-ranked point looks only slightly above the second, even if numerically it's 100× more anomalous. (2) Breaks in streaming (ranks require the full sample). (3) Introduces ties on discrete-valued scores.

**Q5. Why doesn't max-combination's FPR decrease with more detectors?**

*Answer.* If each detector's FPR at its own threshold is $\alpha$ and they're independent, $\Pr(\text{at least one fires}) = 1 - (1-\alpha)^K \to 1$ as $K$ grows. Max is union-of-fires, so it's an OR — combined FPR approaches 1 unless you *raise* per-detector thresholds as $K$ grows (e.g., Bonferroni correction). Averaging is an intersection-ish operation under concentration — false positives need many detectors to agree, so FPR *falls*.

#### Applied

**Q6. Design an ensemble for a credit-card fraud detection pipeline.**

*Answer.* Layered approach. (1) **Cheap per-transaction detectors**: Z-score on transaction amount per user (contextual), IQR on time-between-transactions. (2) **Joint detectors**: Isolation Forest on the multivariate transaction feature vector. (3) **Behavioral detectors**: autoencoder on user-session embeddings. (4) **Graph detectors**: LOF in user-merchant embedding space to catch local anomalies. Normalize all scores to rank or probability; combine by rank-averaging as the default score; route top-$k$ to reviewers. If labels exist, train a stacking classifier (GBM) on top of detector outputs for a final risk score. Monitor per-detector drift; recalibrate monthly.

**Q7. How do you test whether your ensemble is adding value?**

*Answer.* Several checks. (1) Compute pairwise rank correlation between detector scores; if most pairs are > 0.95, you have redundancy. (2) Ablation: remove each detector in turn and evaluate against a labeled validation set (AUROC, PR-AUC, Precision@K). If removing a detector doesn't degrade performance, drop it. (3) Diversity metrics: "disagreement" (fraction of points where detectors produce conflicting top-$k$ flags) should be nontrivial. (4) Cost-benefit: runtime increase vs. performance gain.

#### Debugging & failure-mode

**Q8. Your ensemble's score is dominated by one detector. What's happening?**

*Answer.* Almost always a normalization issue — that detector's raw score range is larger, so in averaging it swamps others. Fix: rank-normalize or Z-normalize all scores before combining. Secondary possibility: the dominant detector is actually the best, and others are noise; check by running each detector alone against labels.

**Q9. Feature bagging hurts performance on your dataset. Why might that be?**

*Answer.* Likely the signal is inherently multivariate across most features, so random subsets that drop critical features lose the anomaly. Possible causes: (a) low intrinsic dimensionality — anomalies are visible only in specific directions unlikely to be randomly selected; (b) feature correlation such that no subset captures the key contrast. Remedies: increase subset size; use importance-weighted subsetting; or switch to model-level ensembling (different base detectors).

#### Probing follow-ups

**Q10. Is there a theoretical result guaranteeing ensembles of anomaly detectors are better than individuals?**

*Answer.* Not a clean one comparable to classification ensemble theorems. The closest results: (a) bias-variance decomposition of the average score (reduces variance, doesn't change bias); (b) concentration of max-based combinations under diversity assumptions. In practice, ensembles usually help, but there's no anomaly-detection analog of the Condorcet Jury Theorem because "truth" is unobserved at train time.

**Q11. How do you handle detectors with different latency?**

*Answer.* Run cheap detectors synchronously on every point; run expensive ones asynchronously, using their scores to supplement/override when they arrive. Treat the ensemble as a streaming update: the score evolves as slower detectors weigh in. Prioritize cheap + diverse for the real-time path, expensive + precise for the offline path. Maintain per-detector SLA monitoring.

**Q12. Could you ensemble at the feature-engineering level rather than the score level?**

*Answer.* Yes — train a single iForest on the concatenation of features plus aggregated statistics (rolling means, differences, residuals from AE). This "feature ensemble" lets the base detector combine evidence. Trade-off: one strong detector on rich features vs. many weak detectors on raw features. Both approaches are common; a hybrid (rich features + model ensemble) often works best.

## 6. Time-Series Anomaly Detection: Seasonal Decomposition, STL, Change Points

### 6.1 Motivation & Intuition

Time-series anomaly detection differs fundamentally from tabular detection because **context matters**. A value of 100 is normal at 3pm and alarming at 3am if the underlying series has a daily cycle. Detecting anomalies in such data requires first understanding and removing the predictable structure — trend, seasonality — and then examining the residual for deviations.

**Two distinct problems.**

1. **Pointwise/contextual anomalies.** A single timestamp's value is anomalous in context. Requires a model of expected value at that time.
2. **Change points.** The *regime* of the series changed — the mean shifted, variance grew, a new seasonality emerged. Individual observations may not stand out but the joint distribution has.

**Real-world ML use cases.**

- **Infrastructure monitoring.** CPU, latency, error rate with strong daily/weekly cycles; anomaly detection must subtract seasonality to avoid flooding alerts every Monday morning.
- **Business KPI monitoring.** Daily active users, revenue — flag real drops vs. expected holiday dips.
- **Sensor health.** Vibration or temperature sensors with known cyclic patterns; anomalies indicate failure.
- **Finance / macro.** Detecting regime shifts in returns or volatility.
- **IoT fleet monitoring.** Thousands of parallel time series, each needing seasonality-aware detection.

### 6.2 Conceptual Foundations

#### 6.2.1 Additive decomposition

A time series $y_t$ is modeled as

$$
y_t = T_t + S_t + R_t,
$$

where $T_t$ is the **trend** (slow, long-term movement), $S_t$ is the **seasonal component** (periodic, known period $p$), and $R_t$ is the **residual** (noise and anomalies).

Multiplicative decomposition $y_t = T_t \cdot S_t \cdot R_t$ is used when seasonal amplitude scales with trend level; typically you take $\log$ first to reduce to additive.

**Anomaly detection strategy.**

1. Estimate $T_t$ and $S_t$ from history.
2. Compute residual $R_t = y_t - T_t - S_t$.
3. Apply any point-anomaly detector to $\{R_t\}$.

The power of this pipeline: it reduces time-series anomaly detection to the (easier) problem of detecting outliers in a roughly stationary residual series.

#### 6.2.2 Classical decomposition

Moving averages to estimate trend, average deviations within each season to estimate $S_t$, subtract both. Downsides: rigid, requires constant seasonal shape, doesn't handle missing data or outliers.

#### 6.2.3 STL — Seasonal and Trend decomposition using LOESS (Cleveland et al., 1990)

A robust, flexible alternative:

- **LOESS smoothing.** Locally weighted regression estimates $T_t$ and $S_t$ via repeated smoothing with separate bandwidths.
- **Inner loop.** Iteratively refines $T_t$ and $S_t$: detrend, apply cycle-subseries smoothing for seasonality, re-estimate trend on deseasonalized series.
- **Outer robustness loop.** Weights observations by $(1 - (r/6\text{MAD})^2)^2$ (bisquare) to downweight outliers.
- **Outputs:** $T_t$, $S_t$ (allowed to vary slowly over time — important for real data where seasonal amplitude changes), $R_t$.

**Strengths.**

- Handles gradually changing seasonality.
- Robust to outliers via the outer loop.
- Configurable trend and seasonal smoothing windows.

**Assumptions.**

- A known seasonal period $p$.
- Roughly additive structure (or log-transformed).
- Enough data (multiple cycles).

**Breaks when.**

- Multiple seasonalities interact strongly (daily + weekly) — need STL-MSTL or Prophet.
- Abrupt regime changes — STL smooths over them, allocating some of the jump to trend.
- Non-stationary variance uncorrected by decomposition.

#### 6.2.4 Change point detection

**Objective.** Identify timestamps $\tau_1 < \tau_2 < \dots$ where the distribution of $y_t$ changes.

**Three broad families.**

1. **Online / sequential.** CUSUM, EWMA, Bayesian online change-point detection. Detect as the data arrives.
2. **Offline / retrospective.** Given a full series, find the best set of change points. PELT, Binary Segmentation, dynamic-programming solvers.
3. **Hypothesis testing.** Compare pre- and post-window distributions, flag if significantly different.

**CUSUM (Page 1954).** Maintain a cumulative sum of deviations from a target; reset when it returns to zero. A sustained deviation drives CUSUM monotonically, triggering detection.

**PELT (Killick et al., 2012).** Pruned Exact Linear Time: dynamic programming that finds the global optimum of

$$
\sum_{i=0}^{K} \left[ \mathcal{C}(y_{\tau_i+1 : \tau_{i+1}}) \right] + \beta K,
$$

where $\mathcal{C}$ is a segment cost (e.g., negative log-likelihood under a model per segment) and $\beta$ is a penalty per change point. Prunes candidates that cannot be optimal.

**Bayesian online change-point detection (BOCPD, Adams & MacKay 2007).** Maintains a posterior over "run length" — time since the most recent change point. Sharp drops in run-length posterior signal change.

### 6.3 Mathematical Formulation

#### 6.3.1 Classical moving-average decomposition

Given series $y_1, \dots, y_n$ of known period $p$ (even-$p$ case uses a centered MA).

Trend: $\hat T_t = \text{MA}_p(y_t)$ for odd $p$; for even $p$, a 2×$p$-MA.

Deseasonalized: $y_t - \hat T_t$. Seasonal estimate: average of $(y_t - \hat T_t)$ over the same within-period position across all cycles, then center so $\sum_{t \text{ in one cycle}} \hat S_t = 0$.

Residual: $\hat R_t = y_t - \hat T_t - \hat S_t$.

Detection: flag points where $|\hat R_t| / \text{MAD}(\hat R) > 3.5$ (modified Z on residuals).

#### 6.3.2 STL algorithm (simplified)

**Inner loop** (repeat for $n_{(i)}$ iterations):

1. **Detrend.** $y_t^* = y_t - T_t$.
2. **Cycle-subseries smoothing.** Split $y^*$ into $p$ subseries (one per phase). LOESS-smooth each. Concatenate back into $C_t$.
3. **Low-pass of $C$.** Remove the remaining low-frequency drift: smooth $C$, call it $L_t$; seasonal estimate $S_t = C_t - L_t$.
4. **Deseasonalize.** $y_t - S_t$, then LOESS-smooth with trend window $n_t$ to get $T_t$.

**Outer loop** (repeat for $n_{(o)}$ iterations):

- Compute residuals $R_t = y_t - T_t - S_t$, robustness weights $w_t = B(R_t / 6 \text{MAD}(R))$ with bisquare $B(u) = (1 - u^2)^2 \mathbb{1}_{|u|<1}$.
- Re-run inner loop with these weights in the LOESS fits.

**Anomaly scoring.** On final $R_t$, use a robust Z-score (MAD-based) or an ensemble of univariate detectors. Thresholds can be time-varying if residual variance is seasonal itself.

#### 6.3.3 CUSUM

Two-sided CUSUM for detecting mean shifts away from target $\mu_0$:

$$
S_t^+ = \max\left(0, S_{t-1}^+ + (y_t - \mu_0 - k)\right), \qquad S_t^- = \max\left(0, S_{t-1}^- - (y_t - \mu_0 + k)\right),
$$

where $k$ is the "slack" (typically half the smallest shift of interest, in units of $\sigma$). Fire an alarm when $S_t^+ > h$ or $S_t^- > h$. $h$ and $k$ are tuned for expected run length under null (average time to false alarm) and detection delay.

**Intuition.** $S_t^+$ accumulates positive deviations; minor noise gets canceled (floor at 0). Sustained bias in $y$ drives it upward until it crosses $h$. The choice $k = \delta/2$ (where $\delta$ is the shift-to-detect) minimizes expected detection delay for that shift under Wald's analysis.

**ARL (Average Run Length).** Under null, $\text{ARL}_0$ is large; under shift of size $\delta$, $\text{ARL}_1$ is small. Trade-off controlled by $h$.

#### 6.3.4 PELT

Cost function: $\mathcal{F}(y_{1:T}) = \min_K \min_{\tau_{1:K}} \sum_{i=0}^K \mathcal{C}(y_{\tau_i+1 : \tau_{i+1}}) + \beta K$.

Dynamic-programming recursion:

$$
\mathcal{F}(s) = \min_{t < s} \left[ \mathcal{F}(t) + \mathcal{C}(y_{t+1 : s}) + \beta \right].
$$

Naive is $O(T^2)$; PELT's pruning step — removing $t$ such that $\mathcal{F}(t) + \mathcal{C}(y_{t+1:s}) \ge \mathcal{F}(s)$ for all future $s$ — reduces expected time to $O(T)$ under regularity.

**Choice of $\mathcal{C}$.**

- Mean shift: segment-wise negative log-likelihood under Gaussian with fixed $\sigma$.
- Variance change: Gaussian with fixed $\mu$.
- Distribution change: nonparametric (rank-based), e.g., Kolmogorov–Smirnov cost.

**Choice of $\beta$.** BIC-like penalty $\beta = c \log T$ is standard.

### 6.4 Worked Example

**Simulated dataset.** Daily website traffic, 365 days:

- Trend: slow linear growth, $T_t = 1000 + 0.5 t$.
- Weekly seasonality: $S_t = 200 \sin(2\pi t / 7)$.
- Noise: $R_t \sim \mathcal{N}(0, 30^2)$.
- Anomaly at $t = 100$: spike of +800 (DDoS).
- Change point at $t = 200$: trend shifts to $T_t = 1100 + 0.5 t$ (marketing campaign).

**Classical decomposition.**

1. 7-day centered MA gives $\hat T_t \approx T_t$ (with some leakage around the change point).
2. Subtract to get seasonal + residual. Average by day-of-week over all cycles: recovers $\hat S_t$ close to truth.
3. Residual $\hat R_t \approx \mathcal{N}(0, 30^2)$ except:
   - At $t = 100$: $\hat R_t \approx +800$ ⇒ clearly anomalous (Z > 25).
   - Near $t = 200$: because the 7-day MA adapts slowly, the trend estimate wasn't perfectly updated, leaving a transient bias of ~20-50 that decays over ~1 week. Might look like a brief run of moderately positive residuals.

**STL on the same data.** Robust LOESS outer loop downweights $t = 100$ so it doesn't contaminate the trend estimate. Residual picks up the DDoS cleanly as a single spike. The change point at $t = 200$ is partially absorbed by trend LOESS (smooth but slope-adjusted) — residuals may show a small bump but the trend shift itself isn't flagged by a residual-based detector.

**CUSUM for the change point.** Run CUSUM with $\mu_0 = $ recent mean, $k = 0.5\sigma$, $h = 5\sigma$. Around $t = 200$, $S^+$ starts climbing as daily values are ~100 above $\mu_0$. It crosses $h$ roughly at $t = 202$–205, firing a change-point alert. Detection delay: 2–5 days — sooner for larger $h$ and at higher FPR.

**PELT for offline retrospective analysis.** Fit PELT with Gaussian-mean cost. Finds one change point near $t = 200$, producing segments $[1, 200]$ and $[201, 365]$. For $t = 100$ (a single spike), PELT either ignores it (the penalty $\beta$ for a 1-point segment isn't worth it) or finds two tight change points at $t = 100$ and $t = 101$, depending on $\beta$. This distinguishes spikes (bad for change-point methods) from regime shifts (correct use).

### 6.5 Relevance to Machine Learning Practice

**When to use decomposition + residual detection.**

- Strong known seasonality (daily, weekly, yearly).
- Enough historical data (at least 2-3 full cycles, ideally more).
- You want per-point anomaly scores with context.

**When to use change-point detection.**

- You care about regime shifts, not point anomalies.
- Detecting distributional changes (mean, variance, correlation).
- Monitoring model residuals for drift.

**When to combine.**

- Production monitoring often wants both: "something spiked today" and "the baseline has shifted."
- Pipeline: STL to get residuals → threshold-based point detector + CUSUM on the residual stream.

**Alternatives / complements.**

- **Prophet** (Facebook) — handles holidays, multiple seasonalities, smoother for business data.
- **Deep sequence models** — LSTM, transformer forecasters; residuals = anomaly score. Useful for complex, multivariate series.
- **Matrix Profile** — distance-based subsequence anomaly detection; no seasonality assumption.
- **Spectral residual** — convert series to frequency domain, flag points whose spectral residual is large (used by Microsoft's SR-CNN).

**Trade-offs.**

- **Bias–variance.** Stronger smoothing (large trend window) reduces variance but smooths over real changes.
- **Robustness.** STL is robust; classical MA is not.
- **Compute.** STL: $O(T \log T)$ per inner loop; PELT: $O(T)$ amortized. Streaming versions of both exist.
- **Interpretability.** Decomposition is highly interpretable (you can show trend and seasonal components to stakeholders). Change-point methods are interpretable per segment.

### 6.6 Common Pitfalls & Misconceptions

- **Ignoring seasonality.** Raw Z-scores on a seasonal series fire constantly during normal high-season periods. Always decompose first.
- **Using a seasonal period that doesn't match the data.** Hourly data with daily seasonality: $p = 24$. Subtle bugs with $p = 7$ or $p = 365$ depending on granularity. Plot the autocorrelation function first.
- **Not handling multiple seasonalities.** Hourly data has both daily (24) and weekly (168) periods. STL handles one period at a time; MSTL or Prophet for both.
- **Treating one-shot spikes as change points.** PELT and CUSUM are designed for regime shifts, not spikes. Spikes belong in residual-based detection.
- **Forgetting that residuals may not be i.i.d.** Even after decomposition, residuals often have autocorrelation (from misspecification) and non-constant variance. Model residual ACF; use GARCH-style variance modeling if needed.
- **Setting CUSUM thresholds by intuition.** Tune $h$ for a target $\text{ARL}_0$ — the "false alarms per year" constraint is concrete and interpretable.
- **Running STL on very short series.** Seasonal estimation needs multiple full cycles; 1 or 2 cycles gives nonsense.
- **Using additive decomposition on multiplicative data.** Revenue, counts — seasonal amplitude grows with level. Log-transform first, or use multiplicative decomposition.
- **Ignoring holidays and known exogenous events.** Christmas traffic is not an anomaly; it's expected. Add indicator variables or use Prophet's holiday regressors.

### 6.7 Interview Preparation — Time-Series

#### Foundational

**Q1. Why decompose a time series before anomaly detection?**

*Answer.* Direct anomaly detection on $y_t$ is confounded by trend and seasonality: typical values vary with time of day, day of week, etc. A fixed threshold either over-alarms at seasonal peaks or misses anomalies at troughs. Decomposition separates predictable structure from unpredictable noise; anomalies live in the residual, which is approximately stationary and detectable by standard methods.

**Q2. Compare point anomalies and change points.**

*Answer.* A point anomaly is a single timestamp whose value is unexpected given the (smooth) model of the series; it's a short-lived perturbation. A change point is a time at which the underlying *distribution* of the series changes — the level, variance, or seasonality shifts, producing many subsequent observations that are typical under the new regime but atypical under the old. Point-anomaly detection uses residuals from a model; change-point detection uses statistics of pre/post windows.

**Q3. Why is STL preferred over classical decomposition?**

*Answer.* (a) STL's LOESS basis allows seasonality to evolve smoothly over time, whereas classical decomposition assumes a fixed seasonal pattern. (b) STL's outer robustness loop downweights outliers, preventing them from contaminating trend and seasonal estimates. (c) STL handles missing data more gracefully. Classical decomposition is simpler and faster but fragile.

#### Mathematical

**Q4. Write the CUSUM recursion and explain each parameter.**

*Answer.* $S_t^+ = \max(0, S_{t-1}^+ + (y_t - \mu_0 - k))$, $S_t^- = \max(0, S_{t-1}^- - (y_t - \mu_0 + k))$. Alarm if either exceeds $h$. $\mu_0$: target mean (null hypothesis level). $k$: reference or "slack," typically half the smallest mean shift of interest measured in standard deviations; it prevents accumulation from pure noise. $h$: decision threshold, tuned via Average Run Length. Larger $h$ ⇒ longer mean time between false alarms and longer detection delay; smaller $h$ ⇒ the opposite. The reset to 0 ensures the detector tracks the *most recent* departures rather than accumulating old noise.

**Q5. Derive (heuristically) why $k = \delta/2$ is optimal for detecting a known shift $\delta$.**

*Answer.* Under a shift of size $\delta$, the expected one-step increment in $S_t^+$ is $\delta - k$. Under the null, the expected increment is $-k$ (slack pulls down). The detector is most efficient when it is maximally negative-drifting under null and maximally positive-drifting under the alternative, which (by SPRT analogy) corresponds to setting $k$ at the midpoint of the two hypothesized means. Thus $k = \delta/2$. This is derived rigorously via Wald's sequential probability ratio test.

**Q6. Explain the PELT pruning step.**

*Answer.* Standard DP for change-point detection computes $\mathcal{F}(s) = \min_{t<s} [\mathcal{F}(t) + \mathcal{C}(y_{t+1:s}) + \beta]$ in $O(T^2)$. PELT prunes a candidate $t$ if there exists a constant $K$ such that $\mathcal{F}(t) + \mathcal{C}(y_{t+1:s}) + K \ge \mathcal{F}(s)$ for all $s > s_0$. The pruning is valid under the condition that segment costs are sub-additive: splitting a segment cannot increase total cost by more than a constant. Under data assumptions where change points occur regularly, expected runtime drops to $O(T)$.

#### Applied

**Q7. You're monitoring hourly DAU for a social app. How do you set up anomaly detection?**

*Answer.* (1) Data engineering: collect at least one year of hourly data. (2) Model: STL with $p = 24$ to capture daily; add a secondary MSTL with $p = 168$ for weekly. Alternatively, use Prophet with daily + weekly + yearly seasonalities and known holiday regressors. (3) Residual detection: robust Z-score per hour-of-week bucket to allow different residual variances. Threshold at $|z| > 4$ to control FPR. (4) Add CUSUM on daily aggregates to catch gradual regime shifts. (5) Pipeline: nightly re-fit of the model on a rolling year, hourly scoring. (6) Alerting: suppress during known events, require $k$ consecutive flagged hours before paging, route to a queue with context.

**Q8. A team wants to flag drops in revenue. When is a 20% drop an anomaly?**

*Answer.* Depends entirely on context. (a) During a product launch: expected volatility. (b) Same day last year: sanity check against yearly seasonality. (c) Compared to STL-predicted value: if STL forecasts $\$1M$ and actual is $\$800k$, the residual is $-\$200k$; compare to historical residual distribution (robust $\sigma$ of residuals). If the drop is $> 3.5$ MAD-sigma, it's anomalous. Add business rules (minimum absolute-dollar threshold) to filter noise on slow days.

**Q9. You detect a change point in model residuals. What does it mean and what should you do?**

*Answer.* A change point in residuals indicates **concept drift** — the model's systematic error has shifted. Actions: (a) investigate feature drift (are the inputs distributing differently?); (b) investigate label drift (is the target population shifting?); (c) retrain or update the model; (d) if the change is benign (e.g., seasonal pattern not captured by the model), update the feature set. Crucially, don't just suppress the alert; residual-level change points are the canonical signal that the model is losing validity.

#### Debugging & failure-mode

**Q10. STL produces residuals that look non-stationary (heteroskedastic). What's wrong and how do you fix it?**

*Answer.* Common cause: multiplicative dynamics where variance scales with level. Fix: $\log$-transform $y_t$ before STL, so variance becomes roughly constant. Alternative: fit a GARCH model on residuals to model time-varying variance, or use a scale-aware threshold (variance estimated over a rolling window).

**Q11. CUSUM fires constantly in production. What's wrong?**

*Answer.* Likely $\mu_0$ is stale — the true mean has drifted but $\mu_0$ was set months ago. Options: (a) use a sliding-window estimate of $\mu_0$; (b) re-initialize after each alarm; (c) switch to EWMA or Adaptive CUSUM. Secondary: $h$ was set for a different noise level and is now too small; recompute using current residual variance.

**Q12. A user reports that your detector missed an obvious Monday-morning traffic drop. What went wrong?**

*Answer.* Most likely the seasonal component absorbed the drop — STL or classical decomposition attributed the low Monday values to "low Monday seasonality," so the residual wasn't extreme. This happens when anomalies align with seasonal phases. Fixes: (a) use out-of-fold seasonality estimation (fit seasonal on all Mondays *except* the current one); (b) complement with a contextual detector that compares current Monday to previous Mondays only; (c) detect on multiple timescales — raw value, residual, and rolling average.

#### Probing follow-ups

**Q13. What if you don't know the seasonal period?**

*Answer.* Estimate it. (a) Autocorrelation function: peaks at seasonal lags. (b) Spectral analysis: peaks in the periodogram. (c) Automated methods: `find_peaks` on ACF, or STL variants like FindFrequency. Start with domain knowledge (business cycles are usually known), confirm empirically, then apply STL.

**Q14. Compare STL + residual detection vs. deep sequence forecaster + residual detection.**

*Answer.* STL is interpretable, fast, and strong for data with clean additive structure. Deep forecasters (LSTM, TCN, transformer) capture nonlinear dependencies, multivariate interactions, and exogenous features, at the cost of opacity, training time, and data requirements. On large fleets of short univariate series, STL wins on cost and interpretability. On a single complex multivariate stream with known exogenous drivers, deep forecasters tend to win on accuracy. Often both are deployed in ensemble.

**Q15. Can you detect collective anomalies (e.g., a sustained 10-day dip of 5%) with point-based methods?**

*Answer.* Poorly. Each single day's residual might not clear a 3σ threshold, yet the run of consistent negative residuals is clearly abnormal. Fixes: (a) CUSUM on residuals — designed for sustained shifts; (b) require $k$ consecutive residuals above threshold before flagging; (c) use a sliding-window test statistic like a sliding t-test or KS test on residuals; (d) Hidden Markov Model approach where states are regimes. Matrix Profile's *discord* also naturally captures subsequence anomalies of configurable length.

**Q16. How do you handle exogenous regressors like holidays?**

*Answer.* Add them explicitly to the decomposition model: $y_t = T_t + S_t + \beta^\top X_t + R_t$ where $X_t$ are holiday dummies or continuous features. Prophet supports this directly. Not modeling holidays leads to false positives on every major holiday. Prefer extending the decomposition over post-hoc suppression in most cases — explicit modeling gives calibrated forecasts for those days.

## 7. Cross-Cutting Interview Preparation

These questions synthesize across all six topic areas and are common at applied-scientist interviews where the interviewer tests system-level reasoning.

### 7.1 Design-level

**CQ1. Design an anomaly detection system for production metrics.**

*Answer sketch.* (1) Inventory: what metrics, what cadence, what alerting SLA. (2) Per-metric modeling: seasonal (STL or Prophet) for metrics with cycles; robust statistics (modified Z, IQR) for stationary metrics; iForest for multivariate. (3) Residual + rule layer: per-metric thresholds set by MAD-based Z on residuals with per-hour-of-week variance estimation. (4) Change-point detection (CUSUM on residuals) to catch regime shifts. (5) Alert routing: suppress known events (deploys, launches), require $k$-of-$n$ consecutive violations, prioritize by severity × business impact. (6) Ensemble for high-value metrics: combine rank-normalized scores from 3+ detectors; calibrate against labeled historical incidents if any exist. (7) Monitoring of the detectors themselves: drift of their false-positive rate, staleness of baselines, recalibration cadence. (8) UX: dashboards with decomposition plots, top contributing features, neighbor comparisons.

**CQ2. You're asked to cut alert noise by 50% without missing real incidents. What do you do?**

*Answer.* (a) Audit alert history: classify each alert as TP/FP; compute per-detector precision. (b) Identify detectors with precision < 50% and consider disabling or recalibrating. (c) Tighten thresholds on noisy detectors; use dynamic thresholds (per-hour-of-week) rather than static. (d) Require consensus: ensemble rank-average with $k$-of-$n$ confirmation before paging. (e) Suppress known scheduled events (deploys, launches, holidays) via an event calendar. (f) Introduce severity tiers: high-precision detectors page immediately; lower-precision go to a queue. (g) Continuously measure the impact and iterate.

**CQ3. How would you detect anomalies in a fleet of 10,000 similar time series (e.g., per-user metrics)?**

*Answer.* Fleet anomaly detection is its own discipline. Approaches: (1) Per-series model with shared hyperparameters — fit a lightweight STL or EWMA per user, anomaly = residual. (2) Hierarchical / pooled models — cluster users into cohorts, fit shared model per cohort with user-specific offsets. (3) Cross-sectional: at each timestamp, compute the distribution of values across users and flag user-timestamps that are outliers relative to the fleet. (4) Matrix-profile on flattened per-user series. (5) Deep models: a shared LSTM forecaster with per-user embedding. Choice depends on compute budget, interpretability needs, and whether "unusual relative to self" or "unusual relative to peers" is the target.

### 7.2 Tricky theory

**CQ4. Why is anomaly detection inherently hard to evaluate?**

*Answer.* (a) No ground truth at training time. (b) Anomalies are rare and diverse; test sets are biased samples of what we can enumerate. (c) The definition of "anomaly" depends on deployment context, which shifts over time. (d) Metrics like AUROC are overly optimistic under severe class imbalance; PR-AUC and Precision@K are preferred. (e) A system can look good on benchmarks and fail on novel anomaly types entirely. Honest evaluation requires labeled historical incidents, prospective testing, and qualitative review with domain experts.

**CQ5. Is the distinction between "anomaly detection," "novelty detection," and "outlier detection" meaningful?**

*Answer.* Partially. In strict usage: **outlier detection** = unsupervised, finding unusual points in a contaminated dataset. **Novelty detection** = semi-supervised, fitting a model on clean data and flagging new points that don't fit. **Anomaly detection** = umbrella term, often implying a production/operational context. In practice the terms are used interchangeably, but the training-time assumption (clean vs. contaminated) does matter — it determines whether you use iForest (unsupervised) or OC-SVM (typically novelty).

**CQ6. Why does unsupervised evaluation sometimes disagree with supervised evaluation?**

*Answer.* Unsupervised methods are judged by how their scores correlate with a notion of rareness or geometric deviation. The real-world "anomalies" a business cares about (fraud, failures, bugs) may or may not be rare in that geometric sense — they may be in-distribution but operationally adversarial. An unsupervised detector may assign a high score to a genuinely rare-but-benign point, and a low score to an operationally bad but geometrically typical one. This is why labeling even a small anomaly set and evaluating supervised metrics (PR-AUC, Precision@K) is crucial for production systems.

### 7.3 Practical probes

**CQ7. A senior engineer claims "just use a neural network for anomaly detection." How do you respond?**

*Answer.* Respectfully disagree in most tabular, low-label regimes. Neural approaches (autoencoders, VAE, deep SVDD) shine on high-dimensional structured data (images, text, sensor arrays) with sufficient normal data. For typical business metrics, iForest or STL + robust residual detection is faster to train, easier to explain, competitive on accuracy, and cheaper to maintain. That said, in deep-heterogeneous multivariate scenarios, deep methods can find signals tabular methods cannot. The right answer is empirical: benchmark candidates against a labeled validation set; pick the one that wins on the metrics that matter (precision@K at the operating point).

**CQ8. How do you deal with anomaly detection in a heavily drifting environment?**

*Answer.* Accept that drift is normal and detectors must adapt. Options: (a) rolling reference windows — retrain or re-estimate $\mu, \sigma$ on the last $T$ days. (b) online methods (EWMA, incremental iForest, online OC-SVM). (c) stratify by context (weekday/weekend, region) so drift within one context doesn't pollute others. (d) Separate anomaly detectors for the *feature distribution* (drift detection) from anomaly detectors for *observation outliers*; the two should inform each other but serve different goals. (e) Monitor detector performance continuously; page when precision or recall on a labeled dribble of known events degrades.

**CQ9. How do you explain an anomaly flag to a non-technical stakeholder?**

*Answer.* Three-layer narrative. (1) *What:* "We flagged $X$ on $date$ because it was $Y$% below our expected range." (2) *Why:* show the decomposition — "Our forecast was $A$ based on trend + seasonality; actual was $B$; residual is this many standard deviations from typical." (3) *Confidence:* "Multiple independent detectors agreed; historically, events like this have been real $Z$% of the time." Avoid jargon. Use plots: the time series with forecast overlay, and the residual distribution with this point highlighted.

### 7.4 Red-team questions

**CQ10. Could an attacker evade your anomaly detector?**

*Answer.* Often yes. (a) *Mimicry attacks* — attackers shape their behavior to stay within the learned normal manifold. Autoencoders and density-based detectors are particularly vulnerable. (b) *Gradual drift* — slow changes that the detector adapts to without alarming. (c) *Feature spoofing* — adversary controls some features and sets them to their known-normal values. Mitigations: adversarial training, rate-limiting recalibration, requiring rare-but-trusted signals in flagging, and combining statistical detectors with rule-based defenses. Recognize that static detectors are not a security solution in adversarial environments.

**CQ11. What if the labeled anomalies in your validation set are unrepresentative?**

*Answer.* Two failure modes. (a) *Survivorship bias* — only easy-to-detect anomalies are labeled. Detectors that score well on them may miss harder cases. (b) *Stale label distribution* — labeled anomalies are from old attack types; new types look nothing like them. Mitigations: actively solicit labels on borderline cases (semi-supervised active learning), use human review on a random sample of high-score points to discover new types, and monitor the rate of "unlabeled, high-scoring" points as a proxy for novelty.

**CQ12. Your anomaly detector is used as an input to a compliance process. What extra concerns arise?**

*Answer.* (a) *Auditability*: every flag needs a reproducible explanation — use interpretable detectors or add explainability layers (per-feature contribution, neighbor retrieval). (b) *Fairness*: check that false-positive rates don't vary across protected groups. (c) *Stability*: small input changes shouldn't flip flags arbitrarily; prefer ensembled or smoothed scores. (d) *Review queue*: humans in the loop for high-stakes flags. (e) *Change management*: detector updates go through review; version the models; keep a log of what detector produced each historical flag. Compliance use is where the Z-score's interpretability really pays off.

## Appendix: Consolidated Method Cheat Sheet

| Method | Paradigm | Best For | Weak Against | Complexity |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Z-score | Statistical | Univariate, Gaussian-ish, stationary | Heavy tails, multimodal | O(n) |
| Modified Z / IQR | Statistical / Robust | Contaminated univariate | Multivariate | O(n) |
| k-NN distance | Distance | Low-dim, uniform density | Variable density, high dim | O(n log n) |
| LOF | Distance/Local density | Mixed-density data | High dim | O(n log n) |
| Isolation Forest | Density (tree) | Tabular, high-dim, large | Correlated anomaly axes | O(n) |
| One-class SVM | Density (kernel) | Clean, small data | Contaminated, large n | O(n²)–O(n³) |
| PCA reconstruction | Reconstruction | Linear structure | Nonlinear manifolds | O(nd²) |
| Autoencoder | Reconstruction | Nonlinear, high-dim | Over-capacity, contamination | O(epochs × n × model) |
| Feature bagging | Ensemble | High-dim, noisy | Low-dim | Base × m |
| Score combination | Ensemble | Hedging against method bias | When detectors are correlated | Base × K |
| STL | Time-series | Known seasonality, additive | Multiple seasons, non-stationary var | O(T log T) |
| CUSUM | Change point | Mean shifts | Slow gradual drift | O(T) |
| PELT | Change point | Offline retrospective | Point spikes | O(T) amortized |
